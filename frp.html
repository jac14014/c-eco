Field Research Protocol
How Decisions Actually Happen Under Systemic Risk
A structured interview guide for understanding real-world decision-making before, during, and after systemic disruptions — without presenting solutions or frameworks.
What We Are Actually Trying to Understand
"In your field, when a systemic risk was coming but hadn't hit yet — how did people actually behave? What did they notice, what did they ignore, and what stopped them from acting earlier?"
We are not testing a theory. We are documenting the empirical gap between when risk becomes technically detectable and when organizations actually reorient behavior — if they do at all. We want to know what this gap looks like from the inside.
Why This Matters Practically
Most post-crisis analyses suffer from hindsight bias: they reconstruct decisions as if the outcome was knowable. We want the forward-looking uncertainty — the fog of the pre-crisis moment, when signals were ambiguous, authority was unclear, and the cost of early action seemed higher than the cost of waiting.
If we can map this fog accurately — across energy, finance, infrastructure, law, and governance — we have something genuinely useful: a grounded description of where institutional decision-making actually breaks down, not where theory says it should.
Our Approach: No Framework Pitch
We do not describe c-ECO. We do not mention "Safe Operating Space," "IEX," "TFP," or any technical architecture. These interviews are data collection, not validation of a pre-existing design.
The participant should never feel they are being asked to endorse, critique, or improve a framework. They are being asked to describe their own experience of a difficult decision situation. Full stop.
Method & Recording
Duration 45–60 minutes (flexible; allow spillover if participant is engaged)
Format Semi-structured; follow the guide but pursue specificity when offered
Recording Audio with permission; handwritten notes on non-verbal cues, pauses, hesitations
Transcription Verbatim for key passages; paraphrase for background context
Analysis Thematic coding (emergent, not pre-defined); cross-case pattern identification
Output Anonymized case summaries; aggregated friction map; no individual attribution
Interview Guide
0
Opening & Consent
3–5 minutes | Establish safety, no evaluation
Suggested Opening
"Thank you for making time. I want to be clear up front: this is not an evaluation of you or your organization. We're talking to people across different fields — finance, energy, infrastructure, policy — who've been in situations where a systemic risk was building but hadn't fully materialized. We're trying to understand how those situations actually feel from the inside. There are no right answers. If I ask something that doesn't fit your experience, just say so and we'll move on."
Consent Check
"May I record this? The recording is just for our analysis — you'll be anonymized in any report, and we can pause or stop anytime you want."
Note for Interviewer
Watch for participant's comfort level with specificity. Some will immediately offer a case; others will speak generally at first. Both are fine. The goal is to reach at least one concrete situation by Section 2.
1
Warm-Up — Your Role
5–7 minutes | Context, not content yet
Prompt A — Role Definition
"Can you tell me, in a sentence or two, what you actually do day-to-day? Not your title — what does a typical week look like in terms of decisions you're involved in?"
  • What information flows across your desk (or screen)?
  • Who do you typically need to convince or coordinate with?
  • What's the range of decisions — from routine to exceptional?
Prompt B — Risk Exposure
"In your field, what kinds of systemic risks are people actually worried about right now? Not theoretically — what comes up in real conversations?"
  • How do these risks get discussed? (Numbers, narratives, scenarios?)
  • Who brings them up? Who tends to dismiss or downplay them?
  • Is there a recent example where this worry became more concrete?
2
Specific Case — A Real Decision
10–15 minutes | The core: concrete, specific, granular
Prompt — Case Selection
"I'd like to focus on one specific situation — it could be ongoing or resolved, large or small — where you were involved in a decision about a risk that felt systemic, but the path forward wasn't clear. What comes to mind?"
  • When was this? (Even approximate: "last year," "three years ago")
  • What was the decision about? (Keep drilling: "You said X — what specifically did that involve?")
  • Who else was in the room? Who had authority but wasn't there?
Prompt — The Moment of Recognition
"When did you personally realize this was a serious situation? Not when it was announced or reported — when did you feel it in your gut?"
  • What specifically triggered that recognition? (A number? A conversation? A failure?)
  • Was this earlier, same time, or later than when others seemed to recognize it?
  • Did you say something at that moment? To whom? What happened?
Critical
Push for sensory/concrete details: "What did you see/read/hear?" Avoid abstract summaries like "we realized there was a risk." We need the mechanism of recognition.
3
Timing — When Things Changed
8–10 minutes | The trajectory, not just the outcome
Prompt — Before Recognition
"Looking back, were there signals before your moment of recognition that, in hindsight, pointed toward what was coming?"
  • Who noticed them? Why didn't they reach you, or why didn't you act?
  • Were they buried in data, or visible but dismissed as noise?
  • What would have had to be different for you to have recognized this earlier?
Prompt — The Decision Window
"Once you recognized the seriousness, how long did you have to do something about it? And what does 'do something' mean in this context?"
  • What options were actually on the table? Who controlled them?
  • Was there a point where you felt the window was closing? How did you know?
  • Did you calculate the cost of acting early versus waiting? Explicitly or implicitly?
4
Friction — What Actually Blocked Action
10–12 minutes | The heart of the protocol
Prompt — Internal Constraints
"What made this decision hard to make? Not the external situation — the internal dynamics."
  • Competing priorities: what else was demanding attention/resources?
  • Information problems: too little, too much, or contradictory? Trusted or disputed?
  • Authority gaps: who needed to sign off? Were they available, willing, capable?
  • Reputational/political risks: what would acting early cost you personally or institutionally?
Prompt — Structural Barriers
"Were there formal structures — contracts, reporting lines, mandates, funding mechanisms — that got in the way?"
  • Did you try to use or change these structures? What happened?
  • Was there a workaround? Why did or didn't you use it?
  • Did anyone say 'we can't do X because of Y'? What was Y, specifically?
Prompt — The Moment of Stuckness
"Was there a specific moment when you knew action was needed but couldn't make it happen? Describe that moment."
  • Where were you? Who were you talking to? What was said?
  • What did you do instead of the preferred action?
  • How did you feel afterward? (Don't push hard on emotion, but note if offered)
5
Aftermath & Recognition
5–8 minutes | Outcome and learning
Prompt — What Happened
"How did this situation resolve? Or is it still ongoing?"
  • At what point was the outcome no longer changeable by your actions?
  • How much earlier would effective action have needed to start?
  • Did the organization formally 'learn' from this? What did that look like?
Prompt — Recognition of Failure
"When did the organization — not just you — recognize that something had gone wrong? Was this explicit or implicit?"
  • Was there a report, a meeting, a firing, a restructuring?
  • Did the language change? (e.g., from 'risk management' to 'crisis response')
  • Did anyone take responsibility? Did anyone avoid it?
6
Comparative Reflection
5–7 minutes | Pattern recognition
Prompt — Cross-Situation Patterns
"Is this case typical of how systemic risks get handled in your field, or was it exceptional?"
  • What would a 'successful' early response look like? Have you seen one?
  • What structural or cultural factors make early action more or less likely?
  • If you could change one thing about how your organization handles emerging systemic risk, what would it be? (Practical, not idealistic)
Prompt — Information That Would Help
"In situations like this, what information — if you had it earlier, more clearly, or from a different source — would actually change how decisions get made?"
  • Not 'better data' generally — what specifically?
  • Who would need to provide it? Who would need to trust it?
  • What would they do differently with it?
7
Closing
3–5 minutes | Gratitude, next steps, debrief
Suggested Closing
"Thank you for going into that level of detail. This is exactly the kind of ground-level view we're trying to understand. Is there anything you expected me to ask that I didn't? Anything you want to add that didn't fit into the questions?"
Follow-Up
"If, as we're analyzing this, we have a clarifying question, may we reach back out? And would you like a summary of what we learn across interviews when it's ready?"
Post-Interview
Immediately write field notes: participant's emotional tone, what was easy vs. hesitant, specific phrases to revisit, your own reflexive reactions. Do not rely on memory or transcription alone.
Cross-Case Synthesis
Output Format
After 15–20 interviews per domain (or saturation), produce:
  • Domain narrative: How does this type of decision typically unfold? Where does it get stuck?
  • Friction map: Recurrent blockages, their mechanisms, and where they appear in the decision chain
  • Temporal architecture: Typical timelines from signal to action (or inaction), with variation
  • Structural patterns: Contractual, organizational, or procedural features that predictably impede early response
  • Cross-domain comparison: What is common vs. field-specific? Where do domains interface and fail to coordinate?
Ethical Guidelines
Anonymity: Participants are never named or identifiable by role + organization combination. Use pseudonyms in notes.

No Harm: Do not probe in ways that could endanger participant's employment or relationships. If they hesitate, move on.

Informed Consent: Clarify that this is research, not journalism or legal process. They can withdraw anytime.

Reciprocity: Offer to share aggregated findings. Do not promise individual benefit.

Positionality: Acknowledge that interviewers bring assumptions. Note these in field notes, not to bias analysis.
Glossary
Decisional Friction
The resistance in organizational systems that slows or prevents action even when risk is recognized. Not "bad decisions" but "decisions that couldn't be made."
Recognition Event
The specific moment when an individual or group transitions from "monitoring" to "this is serious." Often retrospective and contested.
Decision Window
The period between risk recognition and loss of effective agency. Variable across contexts; often shorter than assumed in planning.
Structural Inertia
Features of organizations (contracts, mandates, incentives, reporting lines) that persistently orient behavior away from early precautionary action.
Translation Failure
Breakdown in moving risk understanding from one domain (e.g., technical) to another (e.g., financial or legal) where action could occur.
Hindsight Bias
The tendency, after outcomes are known, to reconstruct prior decisions as more predictable than they were. This protocol explicitly resists this.
Background Reading (Not for Participants)
Diane Vaughan. The Challenger Launch Decision: Risky Technology, Culture, and Deviance at NASA. University of Chicago Press, 1996. (Classic on "normalization of deviance")
Charles Perrow. Normal Accidents: Living with High-Risk Technologies. Basic Books, 1984/1999. (Systemic interaction, not individual error)
Karl Weick. "Sensemaking in Organizations." Sage, 1995. (How meaning is constructed under uncertainty)
Atul Gawande. The Checklist Manifesto. Metropolitan Books, 2009. (Simple tools vs. complex failures)
Gary Klein. Sources of Power: How People Make Decisions. MIT Press, 1998. (Naturalistic decision making)
Giff Constable. Talking to Humans. 2014. (Practical customer discovery — adapted here for institutional research)
