Resumo
O direito ambiental e climático contemporâneo incorporou instrumentos antecipatórios relevantes, como o princípio da precaução, a due diligence e a regulação baseada em risco. Apesar desses avanços, tais regimes permanecem estruturalmente limitados para lidar com sistemas não-lineares, caracterizados por efeitos cumulativos e dinâmicas de ponto de inflexão. Nesses contextos, a janela de intervenção eficaz pode se encerrar antes da materialização do dano, gerando uma lacuna entre o reconhecimento do risco e a capacidade institucional de agir. Este artigo propõe o Princípio Pré-Limiar como resposta a essa lacuna. O princípio estabelece que a relevância jurídica deve surgir no momento em que a capacidade de reversibilidade sistêmica começa a se contrair de forma mensurável, e não apenas diante do dano ou da probabilidade de sua ocorrência. Argumenta-se que essa formulação reconfigura o momento de incidência da relevância jurídica, deslocando-o de uma lógica baseada em eventos para uma lógica baseada em trajetórias sistêmicas. Por fim, o artigo analisa as implicações do princípio para a governança ambiental e climática no contexto pós-COP30.

Abstract
Contemporary environmental and climate law has incorporated relevant anticipatory instruments, including the precautionary principle, due diligence obligations, and risk-based regulation. Despite these advances, such regimes remain structurally limited in addressing non-linear systems characterized by cumulative effects and tipping dynamics. In these contexts, the window for effective intervention may close before harm materializes, creating a gap between the recognition of risk and the institutional capacity to act. This article proposes the Pre-Threshold Principle as a response to that gap. The principle establishes that juridical relevance should arise when systemic reversibility begins to contract in a measurable way, rather than only upon the occurrence of harm or the probability of its occurrence. The article argues that this formulation reconfigures the moment at which juridical relevance attaches, shifting legal reasoning from an event-based logic to a trajectory-based logic. It concludes by analyzing the implications of the principle for environmental and climate governance in the post-COP30 context.
Keywords
environmental law; climate change; precaution; complex systems; reversibility; climate governance; c-ECO; Earth System Law.
1. Introduction
International climate governance has entered, in the post-COP30 period, a phase explicitly oriented toward implementation. The discursive shift from normative commitments to practical execution — often summarized as moving from pledges to delivery — reflects the recognition that existing legal instruments no longer fail primarily because of an absence of targets, but because of their inability to produce legal effects within the time in which intervention remains materially effective.
This diagnosis, however, remains incomplete. Although contemporary climate governance has significantly advanced in incorporating anticipatory mechanisms — including the precautionary principle, due diligence obligations, and risk-management structures — it remains structurally anchored in a juridical-temporal logic that is inadequate for systems characterized by non-linearity, cumulative effects, and tipping dynamics. In such systems, the window for effective intervention may close before harm materializes, or even before it becomes fully cognizable as a legal fact.
The Post-COP30 Pre-Threshold Gap
The post-COP30 climate regime recognizes urgency, implementation, and systemic risk, but it still lacks a clear legal doctrine for the moment before collapse: the point at which reversibility begins to contract, while harm has not yet materialized. The post-COP30 legal gap is therefore not the absence of climate commitments, but the absence of a juridical trigger located before irreversibility.
In this context, a specific gap emerges that contemporary legal doctrine has not yet adequately addressed: the absence of a normative criterion capable of determining the moment at which juridical relevance should arise before irreversibility, and not only in the face of harm or the probability of its occurrence. The central problem is no longer merely the identification of risk, but the institutional inability to act within the window in which correction remains possible — a dissociation that may be described as a Recognition–Action Gap: legal systems recognize risk trajectories, but remain structurally unable to produce binding effects at the moment when intervention would still be effective.
Scientific literature on complex systems reinforces the urgency of this reconfiguration. Studies in system dynamics show that critical ecological and climatic processes do not evolve linearly, but display early signals of resilience loss — such as slower recovery after disturbances and increased variability — before abrupt transitions into potentially irreversible states. These findings indicate that the legally relevant question is not only whether harm will occur, but whether the system’s capacity for reversal is being progressively eroded.
It is at this point that the Pre-Threshold Principle, developed within the c-ECO architecture (Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility), becomes relevant. The principle establishes that juridical relevance should not attach exclusively to materialized harm or to the crossing of critical thresholds, but to the moment at which the remaining capacity of systemic reversibility begins to contract in a measurable and technically verifiable manner. By doing so, the principle shifts the trigger of legal consequence from an ex post logic — based on harm — or a probabilistic logic — based on risk — to a dynamic logic grounded in the compatibility of the system’s trajectory with the preservation of its capacity for stabilization.
This formulation does not merely expand the reach of existing anticipatory law. It proposes a deeper reconfiguration: the transformation of the moment at which law begins to operate as a binding force. Instead of reacting to events or evaluating probabilities, law becomes anchored in systemic states and trajectories, conditioning the validity, enforceability, and continuity of legal obligations upon the persistence of material conditions of reversibility.
Informal Academic Validation
The relevance of this approach directly dialogues with recent developments in Earth System Law. In academic exchange with Louis J. Kotzé — one of the leading references in this field — the formulation of the Pre-Threshold Principle was recognized as compatible with the need, widely discussed in contemporary literature, to shift juridical relevance toward moments prior to the transgression of systemic limits. This convergence reinforces that the problem identified here is not isolated, but part of a structural gap still unresolved in the legal governance of the Anthropocene.
This article argues that the Pre-Threshold Principle offers a legally structured response to that gap. It contends that current anticipatory regimes remain insufficient because of their dependence on probabilistic models and discretionary decisions, whereas non-linear systems require legal mechanisms capable of operating on the basis of trajectories and dynamic states. From this premise, the article develops the theoretical foundation of the principle, its distinction from existing legal categories, and its implications for environmental and climate governance in the post-COP30 context.
2. Limits of Contemporary Anticipatory Law: Precaution, Due Diligence, and Risk-Based Regulation
The evolution of environmental law and climate governance in recent decades has been marked by a progressive shift from a purely reactive logic — centered on the remediation of harm — toward anticipatory models designed to prevent risks. This movement has materialized primarily through the consolidation of the precautionary principle, the expansion of due diligence obligations, and the diffusion of risk-based regulatory structures. Together, these instruments have expanded the temporal reach of law, enabling intervention before harm materializes.
Despite this advance, these regimes remain structurally limited in addressing systems characterized by non-linearity, cumulative effects, and tipping dynamics. The limitation does not stem from the absence of anticipatory mechanisms, but from the way anticipation is legally structured: still centered on events and probabilities, and dependent on discretionary decisions.
2.1 The Precautionary Principle and Probabilistic Anticipation
The precautionary principle represents the most emblematic formulation of contemporary anticipatory law. By establishing that the absence of full scientific certainty should not prevent the adoption of measures in the face of risks of serious or irreversible harm, it breaks with the traditional requirement of full proof of harm as a condition for legal intervention.
Yet its structure remains anchored in a probabilistic logic. The activation of the principle depends on the identification of risks, often expressed in terms of the probability of adverse events. Even where uncertainty is acknowledged, the decision-making process continues to depend on assessments of the magnitude and plausibility of risk, thereby preserving significant technical and legal controversy.
Moreover, the precautionary principle operates predominantly as an authorization or orientation for action, rather than as an automatic trigger of binding legal effects. Its application is mediated by proportionality assessments, balancing exercises, and administrative or judicial decisions, introducing an element of discretion that is incompatible with contexts in which response time is critical.
In short, precaution anticipates the moment of intervention, but it does not alter the underlying decisional structure: law continues to react to the possibility of future events, rather than to the present dynamics of systems.
2.2 Due Diligence and the Insufficiency of the Duty of Care
The expansion of due diligence obligations, especially in corporate and environmental contexts, has introduced continuous monitoring and risk-management mechanisms as central elements of contemporary governance. These regimes require public and private actors to identify, assess, and mitigate risks associated with their activities, promoting a form of permanent vigilance over potential impacts.
However, such obligations operate predominantly at the procedural level. They establish duties of care, but they do not condition the validity or continuity of regulated activities upon the state of the affected system. An activity may be considered legally admissible even where its execution contributes to trajectories of systemic degradation, provided that due diligence procedures have been formally observed.
This dissociation between procedural compliance and systemic compatibility reveals a structural limitation: law regulates the behavior of agents, but does not incorporate, in a binding manner, the material conditions that determine the viability of the regulated activities themselves. In contexts of systemic risk, this approach allows formally compliant operations to contribute to the progressive erosion of the reversibility capacity of the systems upon which they depend.
2.3 Risk-Based Regulation and the Centrality of Probability
Risk-based regulation, widely diffused in sectors such as finance, health, and the environment, represents an effort to rationalize regulatory decisions through the identification, quantification, and prioritization of risks. By introducing metrics, models, and classifications, this paradigm seeks to align the intensity of intervention with the magnitude of the identified risk.
Yet this approach remains centered on the probability of adverse events and their potential magnitude. Regulatory assessment tends to focus on future scenarios and estimates of occurrence, which in complex systems are subject to deep and often irreducible uncertainty.
More importantly, probabilistic logic does not adequately capture the dynamics of approaching critical thresholds. Non-linear systems may remain apparently stable until moments close to abrupt transitions, so that the probability of collapse does not increase proportionally or linearly over time. Consequently, risk-based regulation may underestimate situations in which reversibility is already being compromised, even though this compromise has not yet appeared as a high risk in probabilistic terms.
2.4 The Common Failure: An Epistemology Centered on Events and Probabilities
Despite their differences, the regimes analyzed share one structural feature: they all operate from an epistemology centered on events and probabilities. Law anticipates, but it does so by asking “what may happen?” and “how likely is it to happen?”, while maintaining the future occurrence of harm as the principal point of reference.
This orientation is inadequate in contexts in which the central problem is not the occurrence of the event itself, but the progressive loss of the capacity to avoid or reverse its consequences. In non-linear systems, juridical relevance cannot depend exclusively on the probability of events, because the critical moment may lie in the current dynamics of the system — specifically, in the contraction of its reversibility — and not in the future materialization of harm.
It is precisely this limitation that supports the need for a deeper reconfiguration of anticipatory law. Instead of centering itself on the prediction of events, law must become capable of operating on the basis of trajectories and systemic states, recognizing as legally relevant the processes that precede irreversibility.
3. Systemic Dynamics and Loss of Reversibility: The Material Basis of the Pre-Threshold Principle
The insufficiency of contemporary anticipatory legal regimes cannot be fully understood without reference to the nature of the systems they seek to regulate. Environmental and climate law increasingly operates upon systems characterized by non-linearity, interdependence, and emergent behavior — properties that directly challenge the traditional assumptions of predictability and proportionality upon which legal responses are structured.
3.1 Non-Linear Systems and Critical Thresholds
Unlike linear systems, in which variations in parameters produce proportional and progressive effects, non-linear systems may remain in apparently stable states until small changes trigger abrupt transitions into qualitatively different regimes. These inflection points — often described as tipping points — do not represent merely intensified gradual changes, but structural reorganizations of the system.
In climatic and ecological contexts, such dynamics are widely documented. Critical components of the Earth system — such as the Amazon forest, the Atlantic thermohaline circulation, or ice sheets — may undergo irreversible transitions after certain thresholds are crossed, even if the drivers that pushed them toward transition are later reversed. This property, associated with hysteresis, implies that returning to a previous state depends not only on reversing external conditions, but also on the internal integrity of the system, which may already have been compromised.
3.2 The Intervention Window and the Closure of Reversibility
The existence of critical thresholds introduces a decisive temporal dimension for legal governance: the distinction between the moment at which harm becomes visible and the moment at which it is still avoidable. Between these two points lies the window of effective intervention — the period during which corrective action can still preserve system stability or enable recovery.
In non-linear systems, this window does not coincide with the manifestation of harm. On the contrary, it may close before adverse effects become fully observable or legally cognizable. This means that a legal response based on the materialization of harm — or even on the high probability of its occurrence — may arrive too late, when the capacity for reversal has already been significantly reduced or lost.
The central implication is that juridical relevance cannot depend exclusively on events or future probabilities, but must consider the current state of the system and its capacity to absorb and reverse impacts.
3.3 Early Warning Signals of Resilience Loss
Scientific literature provides tools to identify these dynamics before abrupt transitions occur. Among them, early warning signals are especially significant, as they reflect the progressive loss of system resilience.
One of the most relevant phenomena is critical slowing down, characterized by slower recovery after perturbations. As a system approaches a critical threshold, its capacity to return to equilibrium decreases, manifesting in observable statistical patterns such as increased variability and autocorrelation.
These signals do not predict the exact moment of transition, but they indicate that the system is becoming more vulnerable to abrupt change. In legal terms, they provide an empirical basis for identifying the moment at which the capacity for reversibility begins to contract, even in the absence of visible harm.
3.4 Reversibility as a Legal Parameter
Reversibility, in this context, should be understood as a dynamic and measurable property of the system. It is not limited to the abstract possibility of returning to a previous state, but includes the concrete technical, temporal, and economic capacity to reverse or contain degradation trajectories.
This capacity is not static. It may deteriorate progressively as accumulated pressures reduce the space available for corrective intervention. At certain points, reversal becomes unfeasible, whether because of physical limits, prohibitive costs, or timeframes incompatible with system stability.
The transition from a state of reversibility to a state of irreversibility does not occur instantaneously, but through a gradual process of contraction in that capacity. It is precisely this process — and not only the final point of collapse — that acquires juridical relevance under the Pre-Threshold Principle.
3.5 From Systemic Dynamics to Juridical Relevance
Incorporating these dynamics into law requires a shift in the unit of legal analysis. Instead of evaluating isolated acts or future events, law begins to consider systemic trajectories — patterns of evolution that reflect the cumulative interaction of multiple factors.
In this context, the legally relevant question is no longer only “what is the probability of harm?”, but “is the current trajectory reducing the system’s capacity for reversal?”. This shift moves legal reasoning from prediction to the observation of dynamic states, enabling legal response while intervention remains materially possible.
This transition does not eliminate uncertainty, but it changes its role. Instead of justifying inaction — as often occurs in probabilistic approaches — uncertainty becomes treated prudentially, reinforcing the need for intervention when system stability becomes less robust.
It is from this material basis — the dynamics of reversibility loss in non-linear systems — that the Pre-Threshold Principle can be formulated as a legal rule grounded not only in normative values, but in empirically observable properties of the systems law seeks to govern.
4. The Pre-Threshold Principle: Reconfiguring Juridical Relevance in Non-Linear Systems
The preceding analysis shows that the insufficiency of contemporary anticipatory law does not stem from the absence of preventive instruments, but from the inadequacy of its epistemological and temporal structure in the face of systems characterized by progressive loss of reversibility. It is in this context that the Pre-Threshold Principle emerges as a proposal to reconfigure the moment at which juridical relevance attaches.
4.1 Definition and Normative Structure
The Pre-Threshold Principle establishes that juridical relevance should not attach exclusively to materialized harm, nor merely to the probability of its occurrence, but to the moment at which the remaining capacity of systemic reversibility begins to contract in a measurable and technically verifiable manner.
Juridical relevance attaches when the remaining margin of systemic reversibility begins to contract.
This formulation shifts the trigger of legal consequence to a zone prior to collapse — not the point of rupture, but the point at which the continuation of the trajectory begins to compromise the possibility of system stabilization or recovery. It is therefore a rule that operates within the critical interval between apparent stability and effective irreversibility.
Unlike programmatic principles or interpretive guidelines, the Pre-Threshold Principle assumes the nature of a structuring rule of temporal imputation, defining the moment at which legal effects must necessarily be produced. Its function is not merely to guide decisions, but to establish a binding criterion for the incidence of legal consequences in contexts of systemic risk.
4.2 Function: From Event to Trajectory
The principle’s main innovation lies in replacing the unit of legal analysis. Law ceases to orient itself around events — harms that have occurred or risks that have been projected — and begins to operate on the basis of systemic trajectories.
Within this new framework, the legally relevant question is not only whether harm will occur, but whether the current trajectory of the system is compatible with the preservation of its reversibility. Juridical relevance no longer emerges from the occurrence of an event, but from the incompatibility between the continuation of a trajectory and the maintenance of the conditions that allow effective intervention.
This shift implies a reconfiguration of legal temporality itself. Law ceases to react to consummated facts or future probabilities and begins to act in relation to present dynamic states, anticipating not harm itself, but the loss of the capacity to avoid it.
4.3 Legal Nature: Rule, Not Orientation
Characterizing the Pre-Threshold Principle as a rule — and not as a mere orientation — is decisive for its effectiveness. While principles such as precaution operate as optimization mandates, subject to balancing and gradual application, the Pre-Threshold Principle defines objective conditions for the production of legal effects.
This means that, once the contraction of reversibility is verified at legally relevant levels, the normative response does not depend on discretionary evaluation, but must be activated in a binding manner. Legal action ceases to be a possibility conditioned upon judgments of opportunity and convenience, and becomes a requirement derived from the condition of the system itself.
This structure reduces the space for institutional inaction in critical contexts, mitigating the Recognition–Action Gap identified above. By linking the production of legal effects to empirical indicators of resilience loss, the principle creates a response mechanism that does not depend exclusively on human decision-making at the moment of crisis.
4.4 Operational Content: Validity, Enforceability, and Continuity
Incorporating the Pre-Threshold Principle into legal order produces direct effects on fundamental legal categories, especially validity, enforceability, and the continuity of obligations.
First, it establishes a logic of conditional validity. Legal acts — such as contracts, licenses, or authorizations — cease to possess absolute validity and become dependent on the maintenance of material conditions of compatibility with the reversibility of the affected system.
Second, the principle grounds mechanisms of ex-ante unenforceability, under which formally valid obligations cease to be legally enforceable when their execution would aggravate trajectories incompatible with systemic stability.
Third, it introduces the possibility of automatic suspension of legal effects, triggered by indicators of reversibility loss and independent of prior administrative or judicial declaration.
Finally, the principle enables the formulation of trajectory illegality, according to which legal incompatibility does not arise only from the final result of an action, but from its cumulative contribution to the erosion of the conditions that make the system governable.
4.5 Scope: Reconfiguring the Moment of Law’s Binding Existence
The scope of the Pre-Threshold Principle exceeds the introduction of a new criterion for intervention. It entails a deeper reconfiguration: the redefinition of the moment at which law begins to operate as a binding force.
In non-linear systems, the effectiveness of action depends on preserving a temporal window within which intervention remains possible. By linking juridical relevance to the contraction of reversibility, the principle aligns legal temporality with the physical temporality of the systems it regulates.
In this sense, law ceases to be merely an instrument of response to events and becomes a mechanism of anticipatory coordination, capable of acting while correction remains materially viable. This transformation does not merely expand the reach of environmental law; it redefines its operational foundations in the context of the Anthropocene.
5. Structural Distinction: Pre-Threshold, Precaution, and Prevention
A correct understanding of the Pre-Threshold Principle requires distinguishing it from already consolidated categories in environmental law, especially the principles of prevention and precaution. Although they share a concern with anticipating harm, these approaches operate from substantially different epistemological foundations and normative structures.
Without this distinction, the Pre-Threshold Principle risks being reduced to an incremental variation of existing instruments, obscuring its central contribution: the reconfiguration of the moment at which juridical relevance attaches in non-linear systems.
5.1 Prevention: Centrality of Foreseeable Harm
The prevention principle is structured around the idea of foreseeable harm. Its application presupposes an identifiable causal link between an activity and an expected adverse impact, allowing measures to be adopted to prevent the materialization of that harm.
In this model, juridical relevance remains anchored in the event — even if future — and in the capacity to anticipate its occurrence with a reasonable degree of certainty. Legal action is anticipatory only in a temporal sense, but it does not alter the unit of analysis: the focus remains harm as the final outcome.
This approach becomes limited in contexts where degradation occurs cumulatively and systemically, without direct correspondence to isolated events or linear causality. In such cases, prevention may fail to identify the critical moment at which intervention would still be effective.
5.2 Precaution: Anticipation Under Uncertainty
The precautionary principle expands the scope of prevention by admitting intervention even in the absence of full scientific certainty. It shifts attention from proof of harm to the existence of risk, allowing measures to be adopted in the face of plausible threats of serious or irreversible damage.
Despite this advance, precaution retains two structural features that limit its effectiveness in non-linear systems. First, it remains anchored in probabilistic logic: the decision depends on risk assessment, even under uncertainty. Second, its application is mediated by proportionality and balancing, preserving significant space for discretion.
Consequently, the precautionary principle operates as an authorization to act, not as a mechanism that automatically determines when action must occur. Its effectiveness depends on the institutional capacity to recognize the risk and decide to intervene, which may not occur within the required temporal window.
5.3 The Pre-Threshold Principle: Dynamics, Not Probability
The Pre-Threshold Principle breaks with the centrality of event and probability by introducing a logic based on systemic dynamics. Juridical relevance does not derive from the prediction of harm or the estimation of its probability, but from the observation that the system’s capacity for reversibility is being progressively reduced.
This shift entails a transition from an epistemology of risk — centered on future scenarios — to an ontology of the system — centered on present states and trajectories. Law ceases to ask “what may happen?” and begins to ask “what is already happening to the system’s capacity to avoid the worst?”.
This difference is particularly relevant in contexts where the probability of collapse does not increase linearly or perceptibly until moments close to transition, making probabilistic approaches inadequate for the early detection of critical situations.
5.4 From Discretion to Binding Activation
Another fundamental distinction lies in the nature of the legal response. While prevention and precaution operate as principles subject to balancing, the Pre-Threshold Principle establishes a binding condition for the production of legal effects.
Once the contraction of reversibility is identified at legally relevant levels, legal action is not an option, but a requirement. This structure reduces dependence on discretionary decisions in contexts where inaction may result in the irreversible loss of intervention capacity.
By linking the production of legal effects to empirical indicators of systemic state, the principle creates a response mechanism less susceptible to institutional delays and interpretive controversies.
5.5 Comparative Synthesis
The distinction among the three approaches may be summarized along three axes: unit of analysis, epistemological basis, and nature of the legal response.

Prevention Foreseeable harm Linear causality Anticipatory intervention
Precaution Risk of harm Probability under uncertainty Authorization to act
Pre-Threshold Systemic trajectory Dynamics of reversibility Binding activation
5.6 Implication: A New Rule of Temporal Imputation
The distinction established above allows the scope of the Pre-Threshold Principle to be understood. It is not merely an improvement of existing instruments, but the introduction of a new rule of temporal imputation of juridical relevance.
By shifting the trigger of legal consequence to the moment at which reversibility begins to contract, the principle redefines the point at which law attaches in non-linear systems. This reconfiguration allows legal action to occur within the window in which system stability can still be preserved, overcoming the structural limitations of approaches based on harm or risk.
It is from this distinction that the practical implications of the Pre-Threshold Principle for contemporary environmental and climate governance can be assessed.
6. Implications for Environmental and Climate Governance in the Post-COP30 Context
The relevance of the Pre-Threshold Principle is not exhausted by its theoretical formulation. Its main contribution lies in its capacity to reconfigure, operationally, contemporary environmental and climate governance â especially in a post-COP30 scenario characterized by an emphasis on implementation, but still lacking legal mechanisms capable of acting within the window of systemic reversibility.
By introducing a rule of temporal imputation based on the contraction of reversibility, the principle allows systemic dynamics to be incorporated directly into the structure of validity and execution of concrete legal instruments.
6.1 Continuous Governance and the Overcoming of Episodic Logic
Current models of environmental governance are predominantly episodic. Licenses are granted on the basis of point-in-time assessments, climate commitments are defined in fixed temporal cycles, and compliance mechanisms operate through periodic reviews.
The Pre-Threshold Principle introduces a logic of continuous governance, in which the validity and continuity of legal acts remain conditioned upon permanent monitoring of the systemic trajectory. Juridical relevance ceases to be determined only at the moment of the initial decision and becomes dependent on continuous verification of the compatibility between the regulated activity and the preservation of reversibility.
This shift reduces the lag between decision and systemic reality, allowing adjustments to occur at the moment when they remain effective.
6.2 Contracts and Conditional Validity
In the contractual field, the principle allows for the introduction of a logic of systemic conditional validity. Contractual obligations cease to be absolutely enforceable and become dependent on the maintenance of material conditions that make their execution possible without compromising system stability.
This may materialize through clauses that link continued performance to reversibility indicators; provide for automatic suspension in the face of relevant systemic degradation; and allow the reconfiguration of obligations in response to changes in the systemâs trajectory.
More than contractual innovation, this represents a redefinition of the very notion of obligation, which ceases to be exclusively intersubjective and begins to incorporate systemic conditions.
6.3 Licensing and Infrastructure: From Static to Dynamic Authorization
Environmental licensing regimes and the approval of major infrastructure projects traditionally operate on the basis of ex ante impact assessments. Once authorization is granted, the continuity of the project depends primarily on compliance with previously established conditions.
The Pre-Threshold Principle enables a transition toward a dynamic licensing model, in which authorization is legally conditioned upon system evolution; indicators of reversibility loss operate as triggers for review or suspension; and systemic compatibility becomes a continuous requirement of validity.
This approach reduces the risk that formally approved projects become, over time, incompatible with environmental stability without adequate legal mechanisms for their containment.
6.4 Investments and Capital Allocation
In the financial field, the principle introduces a material criterion for risk assessment that goes beyond traditional metrics. The contraction of reversibility becomes a legally relevant factor for the enforceability and continuity of investments.
This implies the reassessment of assets linked to degrading systemic trajectories; the internalization of irreversibility risks in financing decisions; and the redirection of capital toward activities compatible with the preservation of reversibility.
Unlike approaches based exclusively on disclosure or risk management, the Pre-Threshold Principle directly affects the legal structure of enforceability, creating stronger incentives for systemic alignment.
6.5 Reduction of the RecognitionâAction Gap
One of the most relevant implications of the principle is its capacity to reduce the RecognitionâAction Gap. By linking the production of legal effects to empirical indicators of reversibility loss, the principle reduces dependence on discretionary decisions at the critical moment.
Legal response ceases to depend exclusively on the institutional capacity to interpret complex information and act under pressure, and begins to be activated by predefined criteria based on continuous monitoring.
This does not eliminate the need for human judgment, but it changes its role: from deciding whether to act to defining how to act within parameters already established by law.
6.6 From Commitment to Operationalization
The international climate regime, especially in the post-COP30 context, has consolidated an architecture based on commitments, targets, and mechanisms of cooperation. However, it remains in need of instruments capable of converting these directives into automatically actionable legal responses in contexts of systemic risk.
The Pre-Threshold Principle contributes to filling this gap by offering a mechanism capable of translating scientific signals into legal triggers; aligning the temporality of law with the dynamics of regulated systems; and operating before the effective loss of intervention capacity.
In this way, it does not replace existing instruments, but complements them by providing the missing element: a clear normative criterion for the moment at which action ceases to be optional and becomes legally required.
7. Conclusion
The analysis developed throughout this article begins from a central diagnosis: contemporary environmental and climate law already recognizes the existence of systemic risks, but remains structurally unable to produce legal effects at the moment when intervention is still materially effective. This limitation does not result from the absence of anticipatory instruments, but from the inadequacy of their temporal and epistemological structure in the face of non-linear systems.
Regimes based on prevention, precaution, and risk management represent relevant advances in expanding the temporal reach of law. Yet they remain anchored in a logic centered on events and probabilities, in which juridical relevance emerges from harm â even anticipated harm â or from the estimation of its occurrence. In contexts characterized by progressive loss of reversibility, this approach proves insufficient, because the critical moment may occur before harm materializes and may escape traditional criteria of legal activation.
The Pre-Threshold Principle responds to this gap by proposing a reconfiguration of the moment at which juridical relevance attaches. By linking the production of legal effects to the measurable contraction of systemic reversibility capacity, the principle shifts the trigger of legal action to the zone in which intervention remains possible. In doing so, it transforms not only the reach of anticipatory law, but its operational foundation.
This transformation entails three fundamental shifts. First, it replaces the centrality of the event with the analysis of systemic trajectories, allowing law to operate upon dynamic and cumulative processes. Second, it shifts the epistemological basis from probability to the observation of system states and properties, reducing dependence on uncertain estimates of future events. Third, it converts legal response from a discretionary faculty into a binding requirement activated by empirically verifiable criteria.
In the post-COP30 context, this reconfiguration is especially relevant. International climate governance has advanced in defining commitments and mobilizing implementation instruments, but still lacks mechanisms capable of operating within the temporal window in which systemic reversibility can be preserved. The Pre-Threshold Principle offers a response to this gap by translating scientific signals of resilience loss into operational legal triggers, aligning the temporality of law with the dynamics of the systems it regulates.
More than a new instrument, the principle represents a paradigm shift: law ceases to act predominantly as a response to events and begins to function as a mechanism of anticipatory coordination, capable of intervening while correction remains materially viable. In non-linear systems, this shift is not merely desirable, but necessary if law is to retain its capacity to produce relevant effects.
In this sense, the Pre-Threshold Principle should not be understood as an extension of precaution or prevention, but as a new rule of temporal imputation of juridical relevance, adequate to the conditions of the Anthropocene. By aligning legal action with the dynamics of reversibility loss, it offers a pathway to overcome the dissociation between recognition and action, allowing law to act not only in the face of harm, but before the possibility of avoiding it is lost.
