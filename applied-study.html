Applied Study

Pre-Threshold Contractual Governance in Practice
c-ECO Doctrine — Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility
Institutional research initiative (partnered participation)

The c-ECO Applied Study is a research initiative designed to examine how long-horizon contracts can preserve governability over time under conditions of systemic risk and potential irreversibility. It focuses on contractual architectures that remain legally compliant while quietly consuming future decision-space.

Participation is structured as a research collaboration. The Applied Study does not provide legal advice, does not certify outcomes, and does not publish institutional names without formal agreement and mutual consent.

What the Applied Study examines

  • How specific contractual powers can generate lock-in (renewal, expansion, exclusivity, termination asymmetries).
  • How observable “pre-threshold” signals can trigger procedural reassessment before perclusion.
  • How a c-ECO Clause can condition contractual effectiveness over time without disputing validity at formation.
  • How institutions document preserved governability via traceable internal decision procedures.

Core research question:
At what point should a contract remain formally valid — yet lose authorization to proceed — because its execution foreseeably collapses future decision-space?

Participation profiles

c-ECO Adoption Pathway (5 steps)

Step 1 — Scope the contract’s licensed powers
Identify contractual powers (e.g., expansion rights, automatic renewals, lock-in clauses) that could create irreversible outcomes or limit future decision-space if exercised over time.
Step 2 — Define credible pre-threshold signals
Establish clear, observable indicators that trigger a procedural reassessment — focusing on governability signals rather than technical adjudication.
Step 3 — Embed pre-threshold procedures through the c-ECO Clause
Translate the license into contractual language that conditions the effectiveness of relevant powers on reassessment, documentation, and recalibration.
Step 4 — Assign internal institutional roles
Determine internal owners (Legal, Risk, Governance) and decision-recording procedures to demonstrate preserved governability through traceable processes.
Step 5 (optional) — Seek verification via the c-ECO Pre-Threshold Contract Seal
Organizations may optionally pursue third-party verification of governance design. The Seal verifies architecture, not outcomes.
