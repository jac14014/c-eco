Abstract
A juridical trigger before harm
This paper introduces the Pre-Threshold Principle: a juridical rule under which legal relevance arises when systemic reversibility begins to contract in a measurable way, before environmental harm materializes and before conventional legal triggers become effective. It addresses the RecognitionโAction Gap in climate governance: legal systems increasingly recognize systemic risk, but remain structurally unable to act within the window in which intervention is still materially effective.
The Problem: RecognitionโAction Gap
Legal systems recognize risk โ but act too late.
Legal systems already perceive climate and ecological risk through scientific reports, impact assessments, due diligence procedures, and policy commitments. The problem is not lack of recognition. The problem is the structural inability to convert recognition into binding legal effects while reversibility still exists.
Post-COP30 Context
Implementation Gap

Post-COP30 climate governance is oriented toward implementation, but implementation remains constrained by legal instruments that operate too late in relation to systemic dynamics.

NDC Fragmentation

Commitment cycles and nationally determined contributions remain fragmented, periodic and politically mediated, while system trajectories evolve continuously.

Fossil Fuel Deadlock

The absence of a binding pre-threshold legal trigger allows recognized systemic risks to remain politically negotiated rather than legally actionable.

Pre-Threshold Gap

The legal gap is not the absence of climate commitments, but the absence of a juridical trigger located before irreversibility.

The Principle
Legal relevance arises when systemic reversibility begins to contract.
The Pre-Threshold Principle does not merely authorize precautionary action. It establishes a legal rule of temporal imputation: juridical relevance attaches when the remaining capacity of systemic reversibility begins to contract in a measurable and technically verifiable manner.
Measurable

Activated by technically verifiable indicators of reversibility contraction.

Pre-Damage

Located before materialized harm and before the collapse of effective intervention.

Binding

Structured as a rule, not merely as a policy preference or discretionary principle.

Trajectory-Based

Focused on systemic trajectories rather than isolated events or probabilistic risk estimates.

Scientific Foundation
Critical Slowing Down

Systems approaching critical thresholds may recover more slowly from disturbances, producing observable early warning signals.

Tipping Points

Non-linear systems may undergo abrupt transitions into alternative regimes once critical thresholds are crossed.

Earth System Science

Planetary boundaries and Earth system dynamics provide the scientific context for translating reversibility into juridical relevance.

Threshold Dynamics Research

The c-ECO TDR layer supplies the mathematical language for representing threshold proximity, resilience loss and reversibility contraction.

Legal Consequences
Conditional Validity

Legal acts, contracts, licenses and authorizations remain valid only while compatible with systemic reversibility.

Ex-Ante Unenforceability

Formally valid obligations may become unenforceable when execution would aggravate trajectories incompatible with systemic stability.

Automatic Suspension

Legal effects may be suspended by predefined triggers based on reversibility contraction, without waiting for ex post adjudication.

Trajectory Illegality

Illegality may arise from contribution to a systemic trajectory of irreversibility, not only from materialized harm.
