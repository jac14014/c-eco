The Temporal Mismatch

Law intervenes in damage. We intervene before the threshold.

In complex systems, decisions accumulate until reaching a point of foreclosure — the definitive closing of alternatives. By the time damage occurs, Law no longer has anything to govern, only to sanction.

"Legality persists while the capacity to change course is exhausted. c-ECO breaks with the temporal impunity of current legal design."

Jacqueline A. Ennis

Legal Scholar & Researcher

/ LL.M. WashU Law / Boston University / Silvio Meira Institute

Jacqueline A. Ennis is a Luso-Brazilian legal scholar based in the United States and the author of the c-ECO Doctrine (Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility). Her research formulates a legal-contractual framework for pre-threshold governance, developed to address a critical limitation of contemporary law: the prevalence of ex post intervention mechanisms.

Ennis's work reconceptualizes contracts as temporal architectures of decision. Her thesis demonstrates that legally consequential decisions occur mostly during periods of regulatory compliance, exhausting future decision-making space. c-ECO introduces the concepts of temporal validity and pre-threshold responsibility.

The formulation is anchored in over two decades of entrepreneurial leadership in the Brazilian Amazon (1993–2014), an experience that informs her approach to contracts as dynamic governance structures situated in concrete social and territorial ecologies.

Ennis holds an LL.M. in Business Law from Washington University in St. Louis, with additional studies at Boston University, and is strategically supported by the Silvio Meira Institute (ISM).

She is currently dedicated to systematizing the c-ECO Doctrine in a monograph and developing legal models applied to the governance of systemic risks.

Time-as-Status

Limits to Authorization

Earth's biophysical integrity operates as a negative condition of validity: if the system dies, the contract loses its legal purpose.

Adaptive Design

Duty of active monitoring and mandatory renegotiation as prerequisites for social and investor security.
