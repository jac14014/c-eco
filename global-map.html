2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

c-ECO is a systemic coordination architecture designed to transform fragmented climate and socio-environmental risk perception into coordinated system-level responses.
The complete vision envisions a network of 22 Living Labs across 6 continents, operating across 4 functional layers (L0–L3) plus a Global Integration Hub (GIH) in Geneva.
The architecture integrates scientific observation, financial analysis, multilateral governance, and digital infrastructure into a unified operational ecosystem.
Dimension Integrated Function
Scientific Observation Remote sensing, climate modeling, anomaly detection, tipping element monitoring
Financial Analysis Risk pricing, stress testing, insurance coordination, market translation
Multilateral Governance Public policy coordination, regulatory hooks, legal and institutional alignment
Digital Infrastructure AI, digital provenance, distributed ledger where appropriate, digital twins
Total estimated investment — Gamma vision: US$ 100 million+
Timeline: 7 years
Coverage: 6 continents
Global South presence: 50%
Key Innovation:
The Trigger-Function-Protocol (TFP) cycle — a standardized contractual mechanism that translates scientific anomaly detection into financial risk pricing and governance action within 72 hours.

3. THEORY OF CHANGE

3.1 Core Premise

If systemic climate risks can be detected in real time, translated into financial and contractual instruments, and coordinated across scientific, financial, and governance domains through standardized protocols, then the response cycle can be reduced from years to days, with simultaneous scientific, financial, and political legitimacy.

3.2 Logical Chain

INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → OUTCOMES → IMPACT

Inputs: 4-node Alpha prototype + US$5–12M
Activities: Sensing → Integration → Governance → Coordination
Outputs: TFP prototypes, risk models, coordination protocols
Outcomes: Faster detection-to-decision cycles, institutional alignment
Impact: Coordinated systemic response to climate tipping points

3.3 Architectural Hypothesis

If we create a network of living labs combining scientific sensing (L1), financial risk integration (L2), adaptive governance (L3), and a global decision hub (GIH), operating through standardized contractual protocols (TFP), then it will be possible to reduce the detection-decision-response cycle to under 72 hours, with simultaneous scientific, financial, and political legitimacy.
Operational Logic:
c-ECO does not merely collect risk signals. It translates them into coordinated action pathways across science, finance, law, and governance.

4. SYSTEMIC PROBLEM

4.1 Central Diagnosis

"The law, governance, and financial markets see fragments, not systems."
Each institutional domain — scientific, financial, legal, and political — operates within its own logic, data structures, and timelines. These domains lack a shared operational language capable of translating signals across systems in real time.
As a result, systemic climate risks are not perceived as integrated phenomena, but as isolated events. This fragmentation prevents coordinated action, even when early warning signals are already available.
Core Failure:
The issue is not lack of data — it is the absence of a coordination architecture capable of transforming detection into synchronized system-level action.

4.2 Four Structural Gaps

Gap Description Consequence
Detection Gap Early warning systems operate in isolation, without cross-validation across domains Scientists detect anomalies; no coordinated response emerges
Integration Gap No mechanism translates scientific signals into financial, contractual, and regulatory actions in real time Knowledge exists; action does not follow
Coordination Gap Decisions depend on slow, ad hoc negotiations between actors who have never operated together Every crisis becomes a new institutional negotiation
Legitimacy Gap Top-down solutions lack local anchoring; local solutions lack global scale Solutions are either imposed or ineffective

4.3 Current Cycle vs. c-ECO Target Cycle

Scientific Detection Weeks Hours
Publication / Validation Months Continuous (streaming)
Risk Pricing Years or never < 48 hours
Political Decision Years < 72 hours

Strategic Objective:
Collapse the time between detection and coordinated action from years to less than 72 hours.

7. ORGANIZATIONAL MODEL

7.1 Core + Extended Network Principle

Each Living Lab operates under a dual-structure model designed to balance operational continuity with adaptive capacity: a permanent core team and an extended network of affiliated actors.
This structure allows c-ECO to maintain institutional stability while remaining flexible enough to integrate local expertise, respond to emerging risks, and scale across diverse geographies and sectors.
Core Team: 4–10 full-time equivalent (FTE) professionals per lab
Extended Network: 25–50 affiliated specialists, secondees, researchers, and local partners
Design Principle:
Avoid centralized institutional rigidity while ensuring continuity, accountability, and local legitimacy.

8. TFP CYCLE — TRIGGER FUNCTION PROTOCOL

The TFP cycle is the operational core of the c-ECO architecture, enabling the transformation of distributed scientific signals into coordinated, contractually enforceable actions.

8.1 Five Stages

TRIGGER → FUNCTION → PROTOCOL → ARBITRATION → EXECUTION
Stage Actor Action Output
Trigger L1 Labs Detect anomaly Alert + confidence level
Function L2 Labs Translate into risk Pricing, scenarios, contracts
Protocol L3 Labs Activate response Policy and legal framework
Arbitration GIH Resolve conflicts Prioritized decision
Execution All nodes Deploy TFP Contractual activation
Execution Standard:
All stages combined must occur within a maximum of 72 hours.
TIME-LAG TRIGGER:
Activation based on predefined temporal thresholds when detection signals are delayed or uncertain.
