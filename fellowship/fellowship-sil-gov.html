PART I — DOMAIN INTELLIGENCE PROFILE
1. Domain Overview
1.1 — Definition & Scope

Definition: The public administration institutions, regulatory bodies, legislative and judicial systems, procurement frameworks, and intergovernmental coordination architectures whose capacity for evidence-based decision-making, institutional continuity, and enforcement defines the governance envelope for all other c-ECO sectors.

Scope: National and subnational public administration, regulatory agency capacity, legislative and judicial institutional function, public procurement and contracting systems, intergovernmental coordination, public finance and fiscal systems, and administrative law frameworks governing systemic governance.

1.2 — Systemic Relevance

GOV is the meta-domain of the c-ECO architecture. All other sectors operate within governance systems that define monitoring authority, enforcement capacity, institutional response, and the legal architecture of pre-threshold intervention. GOV threshold crossing means all other sectors lose their governance anchor.

2. Historical Evolution
This SIL treats the domain as an evolving systemic infrastructure rather than a static sector. Historical analysis tracks how technical complexity, interdependence, and governance dependence have increased the cost of late intervention.
Analytical Lens

Institutional failure in governance systems — through corruption, regulatory capture, fiscal collapse, or administrative fragmentation — has preceded every major environmental and systemic governance failure documented in the c-ECO case library.

3. Institutional Landscape
Actor ClassSystemic Functionc-ECO Relevance
Executive and administrative public institutions Designs and implements public policy, regulation, and service delivery. Defines the institutional architecture within which all systemic governance operates.
Legislative bodies and oversight institutions Authorizes policy frameworks and holds executive accountability. Sets normative boundaries and resource allocations for systemic governance capacity.
Judicial and administrative courts Adjudicates compliance, liability, and institutional disputes. Provides enforcement backstop and legitimacy for threshold governance interventions.
Regulatory agencies across sectors Monitors, licenses, and enforces standards in specific domains. Operationalizes governance architecture across all c-ECO sectors.
International governance and treaty bodies Coordinates transboundary governance and global public good provision. Defines normative architecture above national governance systems.

5. Secondary Risks
Secondary Risk Architecture

Secondary risks in GOV are transmission pathways through which primary threshold events propagate into adjacent sectors and institutional systems. Fellows are assigned to map cross-sector transmission in supervised case exercises.

6. Emerging Risks
Emerging Risk Profile

Emerging risks represent frontier threshold conditions identified through current monitoring data but not yet integrated into standard governance frameworks. Fellows are assigned to evaluate methodological approaches for early detection and pre-threshold intervention.

7. Cascade Risk Taxonomy
Cascade Logic

Cascade risks map the transmission pathways through which a threshold event in GOV propagates across the 19-sector c-ECO architecture. Fellows are assigned to trace at least three cascade pathways in supervised scenario exercises.

PART III — SYSTEMIC RESPONSE INTELLIGENCE
8. Response Protocols
Response Design Principle

Response design must preserve evidence continuity, lawful authority, and reversibility. Fellows use this material to identify when monitoring, escalation, Safe Mode conduct, or Restoration First analysis becomes appropriate in the GOV domain.

9. Calibration Council Matrix
Calibration Council Function

The Calibration Council matrix for GOV identifies which institutional actors hold monitoring authority, verification capacity, and intervention authority at each SPS level. Fellows use this matrix to structure supervised threshold analysis exercises.

10. Communication Contingencies
Communication Architecture

Communication contingency planning for GOV addresses how evidence is transmitted, verified, and acted upon under deteriorating systemic conditions. Continuity of evidence chains is a governance prerequisite, not an operational convenience.

PART IV — GOVERNANCE & LEGAL ARCHITECTURE
Governance Boundary

Governance analysis asks which institution can observe, verify, decide, fund, and execute without confusing analytical work with unauthorized deployment. This boundary is strict in GOV.

11. International Frameworks
International Governance Layer

International frameworks for GOV define the normative architecture within which national governance systems operate. Fellows are assigned to map treaty obligations, international standards, and multilateral enforcement architecture relevant to threshold governance in this domain.

12. National Frameworks
National Governance Layer

National frameworks for GOV define the regulatory, legislative, and institutional architecture through which threshold monitoring, intervention authority, and restoration obligations are operationalized at the jurisdictional level.

13. Liability Cascade
Liability Architecture

Liability cascade analysis for GOV maps how responsibility for threshold events is distributed across actors, institutions, and time horizons — including the conditions under which strict liability, contributory liability, and state responsibility apply.

14. Data Sovereignty
Data Sovereignty Framework

Data sovereignty analysis for GOV addresses which entities hold rights and obligations over monitoring data, evidentiary records, and threshold assessment information — including cross-border data governance and access conditions.

PART V — TFP CALIBRATION INTELLIGENCE

16. Velocity (ΔV) Indicators
ΔV measures whether the GOV system is moving toward or away from threshold conditions and at what speed. Fellows are assigned to identify rate-of-change indicators and early warning signals specific to this domain.
17. Uncertainty (σ) Intelligence
σ captures uncertainty in measurement, model design, coverage, institutional reporting, and disputed evidence in the GOV domain. Uncertainty does not justify inaction — it conditions the precautionary architecture of intervention design.
18. Reversibility (Lr)
Lr measures remaining capacity to stabilize, recover, or reconfigure before irreversibility emerges in GOV. Fellows are assigned to map the reversibility gradient from fully recoverable to irreversible across primary risk categories.
PART VI — SCENARIO & SIMULATION INTELLIGENCE
Scenario Calibration Notice

Scenario work converts signal libraries into structured exercises for cohort use. Parameters are illustrative until institutional calibration assigns live values specific to the GOV domain context.

19. Stress Test Scenarios
Stress Test Framework

Stress test scenarios for GOV simulate threshold crossing events across primary risk categories. Fellows are assigned to evaluate institutional response adequacy, evidence chain continuity, and reversibility preservation under each scenario.

20. Monte Carlo Parameters
Monte Carlo Framework

Monte Carlo parameter sets for GOV define probabilistic distributions for TFP variables under current evidence conditions. Fellows use these parameters to evaluate uncertainty ranges in supervised threshold calculation exercises.

21. Continuity Recovery
Continuity Architecture

Continuity recovery design for GOV defines the sequence and conditions under which system function is restored following a threshold event — distinguishing emergency stabilization, functional restoration, and systemic rehabilitation phases.

PART VIII — AUTHORITATIVE SOURCE LIBRARY
26. Source Classification
Classification Standard

Class A: Primary authoritative sources. Class B: Secondary institutional and monitoring sources. Class C: Industry, academic, and civil society sources. All sources are subject to methodological fidelity review before deployment in TFP calculations.

27. Class A Sources
  • World Bank governance indicators and public administration assessments
  • OECD regulatory governance and Better Regulation frameworks
  • UN governance and rule of law documentation
  • Transparency International corruption perceptions and institutional integrity assessments
28. Class B Sources
  • National regulatory agency capacity and independence assessments
  • Public finance sustainability and fiscal risk monitoring
  • Administrative courts and judicial independence assessments
  • Cross-sector coordination mechanism evaluations
29. Class C Sources
  • Government audit and inspector general reports
  • Academic public administration and institutional governance journals
  • NGO governance accountability and transparency monitoring
  • Regional governance network benchmarking data
