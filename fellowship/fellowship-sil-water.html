Part I
Domain Intelligence Profile

1. Domain Overview

1.1
Definition & Scope

Definition: The abstraction, treatment, storage, distribution, and reclamation of freshwater, together with wastewater collection, sanitation, stormwater management, and the dams and irrigation systems that govern water at basin scale.

Scope: Surface and groundwater abstraction; desalination; drinking-water treatment (WTP); pumping and distribution mains; sewerage and wastewater treatment (WWTP); reuse/reclamation; dams, reservoirs, and levees; irrigation networks; and the SCADA layer controlling pumps, valves, and dosing.

1.2
Systemic Relevance

Water is life-critical on the shortest clock of any domain — populations have days, not weeks, of resilience. It underpins Healthcare (hygiene, dialysis, hospitals), Energy (thermal and nuclear cooling, hydropower), Agribusiness (irrigation), and Digital (data-center cooling).

Water and Energy form a tight nexus: pumping and treatment are among the largest electricity loads, while thermal generation depends on cooling water. A failure in either can lock the other into a mutual-dependency deadlock — a central reason Water sits adjacent to Energy in c-ECO criticality.

1.3
Key Infrastructures
Dams & reservoirs
Storage + flood control; catastrophic-failure assets (Banqiao, Brumadinho, Derna)
Treatment plants (WTP/WWTP)
Coagulation, filtration, disinfection; chemical-supply dependent
Pumping & distribution
Mains, pressure zones; large electricity load (water-energy nexus)
Groundwater / aquifers
Slow-recharge buffer; depletion & saltwater intrusion risk
Desalination
Drought-resilient but energy- and brine-intensive
SCADA / dosing control
Cyber-physical control of valves, pumps, chlorine (Oldsmar 2021)

2. Historical Evolution (ΔV Calibration)

Milestones anchoring the velocity (ΔV) calibration — from slow-onset scarcity to instantaneous dam failure:

  • 1854 Broad Street cholera (London) — birth of the water-sanitation-health link.
  • 1960s–2000s Aral Sea — slow-onset, near-irreversible basin collapse.
  • 1975 Banqiao Dam failure — catastrophic dam-break cascade.
  • 2000 Walkerton (Canada) — E. coli contamination; treatment-governance failure.
  • 2014 Flint (US) — lead contamination from corrosion-control failure.
  • 2015 Mariana / 2019 Brumadinho — mining tailings-dam disasters (Mining → Water).
  • 2018 Cape Town "Day Zero" — multi-year drought near municipal shutoff.
  • 2022 Jackson, MS / 2023 Derna (Libya) — treatment collapse; dam-break flood.

The full 60-event historical library is in Part IX, Section 34.

3. Global Institutional Landscape

Key institutions with TFP relevance: UN-Water (coordination); WHO (drinking-water guidelines, JMP); WMO (hydrology, drought/flood); FAO (AQUASTAT, irrigation); UNEP (water quality); World Bank (infrastructure, ESMAP/water); OECD (governance); IWA (International Water Association); ICOLD (dam safety); IAHR (hydraulic research); transboundary bodies (Mekong River Commission, Nile Basin Initiative, International Joint Commission).

Glossary, Sources & Classification

Glossary of TFP & Domain Terms

P — Position
Proximity to a failure threshold; higher P = closer to systemic breach.
ΔV — Velocity
Rate of change of system state toward (or away from) breach.
σ — Uncertainty
Confidence interval / governance gaps around an estimate.
Lr — Reversibility Liquidity
Capacity to reverse/recover from failure (0 = irreversible, 1 = fully reversible).
SPS
Systemic Proximity Score — composite breach-proximity indicator driving response levels.
TRS / RLS
Systemic Trend and Restoration/Liquidity sub-scores used in cross-domain calibration.
Day Zero
The point at which a municipal water system can no longer supply taps and shifts to rationed collection points.
NRW
Non-Revenue Water — treated water lost to leakage, theft, or metering error.
WTP / WWTP
Water Treatment Plant / Wastewater Treatment Plant.
CSO
Combined Sewer Overflow — storm-driven discharge of untreated sewage.
SWE
Snow Water Equivalent — water content stored in snowpack.
HAB
Harmful Algal Bloom — toxin-producing bloom (e.g., microcystin).
PFAS
Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — persistent "forever chemicals".
Dead pool
Reservoir level too low to release water downstream or generate power.
Water-energy nexus
Mutual dependence: water needs energy to move/treat; energy needs water to cool.
