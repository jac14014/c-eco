Foundations of Systemic Legal Transformation

Chapters 1–4
Chapter 1 The Transformation of Juridical Temporality: From Retrospection to Pre-Threshold Governance

The juridical order, as it emerged within the intellectual and institutional architecture of modernity, was fundamentally structured around a retrospective conception of legal relevance. Its primary function consisted in the attribution of consequences to acts whose effects had already materialized, been observed, and rendered susceptible to causal reconstruction. Within this framework, legality operated upon completed events, and juridical intervention was temporally positioned after the occurrence of harm.

This configuration is neither accidental nor merely historical; it reflects the conditions under which law developed as a system of normative stabilization. Classical doctrines of responsibility—whether articulated through fault, breach, or strict liability—presuppose a world in which action and consequence maintain sufficient proximity to allow retrospective adjudication to retain corrective efficacy (Kelsen, 1967; Hart, 1994). Legal reasoning, in this sense, is anchored in a temporal structure in which damage precedes relevance, and reparation constitutes the principal juridical response.

Such a model proved functionally adequate so long as the effects of human activity remained localized, temporally contained, and materially reversible. Under these conditions, even delayed legal intervention could restore equilibrium, compensate loss, or reconstitute normative order. The juridical system, though reactive, remained effective because the world it governed was, to a significant extent, recoverable.

However, the progressive transformation of socio-technical and ecological systems throughout the twentieth century introduced dynamics that increasingly strained this retrospective paradigm. The expansion of industrial infrastructures, the intensification of global interdependence, and the emergence of complex, non-linear systems generated forms of risk that no longer conformed to the temporal assumptions underlying classical legal reasoning.

In response, the juridical order began to undergo a gradual yet significant temporal displacement toward ex ante modalities of governance. The first manifestation of this shift can be observed in the emergence of preventive regulation. Particularly within industrial and urban contexts, legal systems began to intervene prior to harm through the establishment of technical standards, licensing regimes, and safety requirements. Here, the objective was no longer solely to repair damage, but to avoid its occurrence. Yet this preventive turn remained structurally bounded: it operated through static thresholds, formal compliance, and predefined limits that did not account for dynamic system behavior.

A second stage introduced risk as a central juridical category. Legal reasoning increasingly relied upon probabilistic assessments, enabling intervention based on the likelihood and magnitude of potential harm. This development extended the temporal reach of law, allowing it to act before damage materialized. However, risk-based governance remained anchored in a calculative paradigm, wherein harm was treated as a quantifiable variable subject to balancing against economic or social benefits.

A third stage, most prominently articulated within international environmental law, consolidated the precautionary principle. Under this principle, uncertainty ceased to function as a barrier to legal action and instead became a justification for intervention (Trouwborst, 2002; United Nations, 1992). Law thus acknowledged that waiting for full scientific certainty could render harm irreversible. Yet even this doctrinal innovation remained conceptually tied to the anticipation of damage. It lowered evidentiary thresholds but did not fundamentally alter the object of juridical concern.

More recent developments—such as compliance regimes, due diligence obligations, and proactive legal frameworks—have further extended this anticipatory orientation into organizational and contractual practice. Legal actors are increasingly required to identify and mitigate risks prior to their manifestation. Nevertheless, these mechanisms continue to operate within a paradigm oriented toward the management of consequences rather than the preservation of systemic conditions.

Across these stages, a consistent pattern emerges: law progressively moves forward in time, yet remains conceptually tethered to harm as its primary referent. This temporal displacement, while significant, reveals a deeper structural limitation. All prior models of ex ante governance presuppose that the systems they regulate remain, in principle, reversible. Whether through prevention, risk management, or precaution, juridical reasoning continues to operate under the assumption that intervention—if timely—can avoid, mitigate, or compensate harm. The underlying temporal logic remains one of recoverability.

However, advances in Earth system science have demonstrated that many critical processes are governed not by linear causality but by non-linear dynamics, feedback loops, and threshold behavior (Lenton et al., 2008; Steffen et al., 2015). The planetary boundaries framework further establishes that human activity operates within a finite Safe Operating Space (SOS), beyond which systemic stability may be compromised (Rockström et al., 2009). Within such systems, trajectories may become self-reinforcing, and the window for effective intervention may close prior to the manifestation of observable damage.

Under these conditions, the anticipation of harm—even when methodologically sophisticated—may occur too late. The juridical problem thus undergoes a fundamental transformation. It is no longer sufficient to anticipate damage; law must become capable of operating upon trajectories as such. The relevant question shifts from whether harm will occur, to whether the trajectory leading toward it remains interruptible within the available margin of reversibility.

This shift marks the emergence of a new temporal structure of juridical relevance. Within the c-ECO framework, this transformation is articulated through the transition from ex ante governance to pre-threshold governance. Unlike prior anticipatory models, which operate upon probabilistic estimations of harm, pre-threshold governance is grounded in the continuous monitoring of systemic trajectories through certified variables: Position (P), Velocity (ΔV), Uncertainty (σ), and Reversibility Liquidity (Lr). These variables do not describe hypothetical outcomes but reflect the real-time condition of the system relative to the Safe Operating Space (SOS).

Reversibility Liquidity (Lr), in particular, becomes the critical juridical parameter. It measures the capacity of a system to arrest or reverse adverse trajectories within the relevant temporal window. When Lr approaches depletion, the system enters a condition in which further intervention may no longer restore prior states. At this point, juridical reasoning cannot rely on damage, risk, or precaution as sufficient categories.

It is precisely in this context that the notion of systemic incompatibility of execution arises. Acts that remain formally valid under existing legal standards may nevertheless become materially non-viable when their certified trajectory indicates convergence toward irreversible system states. The juridical issue is no longer whether the act complies with regulatory norms or contractual obligations, but whether its continued execution remains compatible with the preservation of the Safe Operating Space.

Legal validity, therefore, undergoes a profound transformation. It ceases to function as a static attribute determined at the moment of formation and becomes instead a dynamic condition contingent upon continuous systemic compatibility. The validity, enforceability, and continuity of juridical relations are no longer guaranteed by formal compliance alone, but are conditioned upon the preservation of reversibility and system stability.

This reconfiguration introduces a qualitatively distinct mode of legal operation. Through the Threshold Function Protocol (TFP), legal effects are automatically triggered based on certified systemic conditions. When Position (P), Velocity (ΔV), Uncertainty (σ), and Reversibility Liquidity (Lr) indicate that a trajectory is approaching or exceeding pre-defined thresholds, juridical consequences arise ex lege, without the need for discretionary intervention. This automaticity does not eliminate juridical control but repositions it within a framework of verification, auditability, and certified data integrity.

Within this architecture, instruments such as Ex-Ante Unenforceability (IEX) and Systemic Perclusion operate as mechanisms of pre-threshold intervention. IEX suspends the enforceability of obligations whose execution would generate irreversible trajectories, while Systemic Perclusion prevents the continuation of formally lawful acts that contribute to systemic destabilization.

The evidentiary foundation of this regime is provided by Systemic Proof, which replaces interpretative or probabilistic evidence with certified, sensor-based, and continuously updated data validated through institutional structures such as the Data, Verification & Certification Body (DVB). Legal relevance is thus grounded in measurable system states rather than retrospective reconstruction or predictive speculation.

This transformation does not abolish prior legal forms but reorders them within a new hierarchy. Retrospective liability, preventive regulation, risk governance, and precautionary principles remain operative, but are subordinated to the material primacy of the Earth System. Where conflict arises, systemic compatibility prevails as a condition of juridical operability.

The evolution of juridical reason thus reveals a progressive displacement along the temporal axis: from ex post reparation, to ex ante prevention, to risk-based anticipation, and ultimately to pre-threshold governance. The c-ECO framework marks a decisive rupture within this trajectory. For the first time, law operates not upon events or probabilities, but upon the real-time condition of the systems within which those events may unfold. Juridical temporality is no longer defined by the sequence of act and consequence, but by the dynamics of trajectories and the limits of reversibility.

In this sense, the transition is not merely functional but ontological. Law ceases to be a system that reacts to what has occurred or anticipates what may occur, and becomes instead a system that governs the conditions under which occurrence itself remains possible. Planetary limits are no longer external constraints upon legal autonomy; they become the material boundaries of juridical existence.

Chapter 2 Systemic Risk, Irreversibility, and the Limits of Causality

The transformation of juridical temporality outlined in the preceding chapter is inseparable from a deeper shift in the nature of the phenomena to which law is now required to respond. The increasing prevalence of systemic risk within contemporary socio-technical and ecological systems exposes the structural limitations of causality as the primary organizing principle of legal reasoning.

Classical juridical frameworks are grounded in a model of causation that presupposes linearity, separability, and temporal proximity. Within this model, legal responsibility is constructed through the identification of discrete acts producing identifiable effects, allowing liability to be attributed on the basis of demonstrable causal links between conduct and harm (Hart, 1994; Kelsen, 1967). This structure underpins doctrines of fault, negligence, and strict liability, all of which depend upon the capacity to reconstruct causal chains with sufficient precision to justify normative intervention.

Such a model remains effective in contexts where interactions are relatively simple, effects are localized, and temporal lags between cause and consequence are limited. Under these conditions, causality functions as a reliable bridge between action and juridical response. However, this assumption progressively collapses as systems increase in complexity.

The emergence of complex systems—characterized by interdependence, feedback loops, and non-linear dynamics—renders causal attribution increasingly unstable. In such systems, outcomes do not arise from isolated acts but from the interaction of multiple variables distributed across time and space. Causal chains become diffuse, overlapping, and often indeterminate, undermining the epistemic conditions required for traditional legal attribution (Helbing, 2013; May, Levin and Sugihara, 2008; Scheffer et al., 2009).

This transformation is particularly evident in Earth system processes. Climate dynamics, biodiversity loss, hydrological cycles, and energy infrastructures operate as coupled systems in which incremental pressures may trigger disproportionate and abrupt changes once critical thresholds are approached. These dynamics are governed not by linear accumulation but by tipping behavior, in which gradual variation gives way to rapid and self-reinforcing transformation (Lenton et al., 2008; Steffen et al., 2015).

Within this context, the concept of risk undergoes a qualitative transformation. Systemic risk cannot be understood as the aggregation of individual risks, but rather as an emergent property arising from interactions, feedback loops, and path-dependent processes that cannot be reduced to independent probabilistic events (Taleb et al., 2014). As a consequence, risk ceases to be fully capturable through models that assume statistical regularity and independence.

From a juridical perspective, this poses a fundamental problem. If harm arises from distributed interactions rather than identifiable acts, the attribution of responsibility through causal reconstruction becomes inherently unstable. Legal systems grounded in causality encounter conditions in which the very criteria required to establish liability—clarity, proximity, and determinacy—are systematically eroded (Mayer, 2016).

This does not render causality irrelevant. Rather, it reveals its insufficiency as a standalone foundation for juridical relevance in systems governed by non-linear dynamics.

The inadequacy of causality becomes decisive when irreversibility is introduced as a governing condition of system behavior. Irreversibility designates a state in which system transformations, once initiated beyond critical thresholds, cannot be reversed within meaningful temporal, economic, or technological horizons (Gardiner, 2011). In such cases, the distinction between prevention and reparation collapses, as restoration becomes materially unattainable.

Scientific research has identified multiple domains in which such irreversible transitions may occur, including ice sheet collapse, ecosystem dieback, and disruptions of oceanic circulation patterns (Rockström et al., 2009; Steffen et al., 2015). These processes are governed by threshold dynamics in which gradual pressures accumulate until a tipping point is reached, beyond which change becomes self-reinforcing and independent of initial drivers (Lenton et al., 2008).

Within the c-ECO framework, irreversibility is operationalized through the concept of Reversibility Liquidity (Lr), defined as the system's capacity to arrest or reverse adverse trajectories within the relevant temporal window. Reversibility Liquidity is not a binary condition but a continuous variable subject to measurement, monitoring, and certification.

As Reversibility Liquidity declines, the system approaches a state in which intervention progressively loses effectiveness. Trajectories become increasingly resistant to correction, and the temporal window for meaningful action contracts. At sufficiently low levels of Reversibility Liquidity, intervention ceases to be capable of restoring prior system states.

The juridical significance of this condition is profound. Where classical legal reasoning attaches relevance to the occurrence of harm, the c-ECO framework shifts the locus of juridical concern to the depletion of reversibility. Legal relevance arises not at the moment damage materializes, but when the capacity to prevent irreversible transformation begins to collapse. In this sense, irreversibility precedes harm as the critical threshold for legal intervention.

This inversion fundamentally alters the role of causality. If juridical action is triggered by the depletion of Reversibility Liquidity rather than by the occurrence of damage, the necessity of reconstructing precise causal chains between individual acts and specific harms is substantially reduced. Instead, juridical reasoning operates upon certified system conditions, focusing on whether ongoing conduct contributes to trajectories that erode reversibility.

The central question thus shifts: not who caused harm, but whether continued execution remains compatible with the preservation of the Safe Operating Space.

Systemic risk, as conceptualized within the c-ECO framework, therefore transcends the aggregation of individual risks and must be understood as a property of system dynamics rather than isolated events (May, Levin and Sugihara, 2008; Scheffer et al., 2009). This reconceptualization translates advances in complexity science into juridically operative categories.

The Safe Operating Space, as identified within Earth system science, constitutes the set of conditions within which system stability can be maintained (Rockström et al., 2009). Once trajectories exceed these boundaries, system behavior may shift abruptly, rendering prior states unattainable. Legal systems that fail to internalize these constraints operate under assumptions that no longer correspond to material reality.

The limits of conventional causality become particularly evident in the presence of positive feedback mechanisms. The Arctic ice-albedo feedback, for example, generates self-reinforcing dynamics in which initial warming reduces ice cover, decreasing albedo and accelerating further warming. Within such recursive processes, causal attribution becomes diffuse and indeterminate, as outcomes emerge from system behavior rather than isolated acts (Lenton et al., 2008).

Under these conditions, juridical reliance on linear causation becomes structurally insufficient. Legal reasoning must therefore shift from reconstructing causal chains to evaluating whether ongoing trajectories remain compatible with the preservation of the Safe Operating Space and the maintenance of Reversibility Liquidity.

This reconfiguration does not eliminate responsibility but transforms its structure. Responsibility becomes distributed, dynamic, and forward-looking. Legal actors are no longer judged solely on the basis of past conduct, but on their participation in trajectories that may lead to systemic destabilization. Formal compliance with existing norms becomes insufficient where such compliance coexists with trajectories that undermine reversibility (Kotzé and Kim, 2019).

The limits of causality are therefore structural rather than merely epistemic. Legal systems that rely exclusively on causal reconstruction are inherently reactive and temporally delayed. They intervene only after the conditions necessary for effective intervention may have already been lost. In systems governed by threshold dynamics, this delay is not merely inefficient; it renders intervention materially ineffective.

The c-ECO framework responds to this limitation through the integration of trajectory-based variables into juridical reasoning. The Threshold Function Protocol (TFP) operates through four certified variables—Position (P), Velocity (ΔV), Uncertainty (σ), and Reversibility Liquidity (Lr)—which together determine the system's condition relative to the Safe Operating Space. Position measures proximity to critical thresholds, Velocity captures the direction and rate of change, Uncertainty reflects epistemic confidence in the available data, and Reversibility Liquidity indicates the remaining capacity for recovery.

Through the Threshold Function Protocol, these variables are translated into juridically operative classifications. When certified system conditions indicate convergence toward irreversibility, legal effects are automatically triggered without the need for discretionary causal attribution. Juridical intervention thus occurs prior to the loss of reversibility rather than in response to realized harm.

Within this architecture, systemic risk is no longer an object of probabilistic management but a condition of continuous governance. Legal relevance attaches to trajectories rather than events, and intervention is structured around the preservation of system stability rather than the attribution of past responsibility.

The displacement of causality by trajectory does not signify the abandonment of legal reasoning, but its reconfiguration. Causality remains relevant as an analytical tool, particularly for retrospective accountability. However, it is subordinated to a broader framework in which the preservation of reversibility and systemic stability constitutes the primary juridical objective.

This transformation reflects a fundamental shift in the logic of law. Law ceases to function primarily as a system that reacts to causes and effects, and becomes instead a system that governs the evolution of states within complex systems. Juridical relevance is no longer anchored in discrete events but in the dynamic condition of systems over time.

This shift is not optional. It is imposed by the material characteristics of the systems within which law now operates. Where irreversibility defines the boundary of intervention, and where systemic risk cannot be reduced to linear causation, juridical reasoning must evolve accordingly. The limits of causality mark the threshold beyond which law must abandon its exclusive reliance on retrospective reconstruction and adopt a mode of operation capable of engaging with trajectories in real time.

It is precisely at this threshold that the c-ECO framework situates itself.

Chapter 3 From Damage to Trajectory: The Emergence of Predictive Legality

The emergence of predictive legality marks a fundamental doctrinal innovation within the c-ECO framework. Rather than conditioning legal intervention upon realized harm, this approach activates juridical effects on the basis of certified systemic trajectories approaching critical thresholds (Wiener, 2011; Peel and Osofsky, 2018). This shift from damage-based to trajectory-based governance responds to the temporal asymmetry characteristic of Earth system processes, in which the window for effective intervention may close prior to the materialization of observable harm.

Predictive legality does not merely extend anticipatory doctrines; it redefines the object of juridical relevance. Legal concern is no longer centered on discrete acts or isolated consequences, but on the dynamic evolution of system states over time. Under this framework, the relevant question is not whether harm has occurred or may occur, but whether ongoing trajectories remain compatible with the preservation of system stability, reversibility, and long-term habitability.

In this context, the notion traditionally described as "trajectory illegality" is more precisely articulated within the c-ECO framework as a condition of systemic incompatibility of execution. Acts that remain formally lawful under prevailing regulatory and contractual standards may nevertheless become materially non-viable when their continued execution contributes to trajectories that approach or exceed systemic thresholds. Juridical relevance thus shifts from the static evaluation of acts to the dynamic assessment of trajectories.

This condition does not abolish the distinction between lawful and unlawful conduct but supplements it with an additional dimension. Beyond formal legality and classical illegality, a third juridical state emerges: that of systemically incompatible execution. In this state, conduct is not necessarily unlawful in the traditional sense, but its continuation is incompatible with the preservation of the conditions under which legal relations themselves remain operable.

The evidentiary foundation of this transformation rests upon advances in Earth system monitoring, data integration, and real-time analysis. As Reid et al. (2010) observe, the capacity to detect early warning signals of critical transitions has significantly improved, enabling the identification of trajectories approaching tipping points. These developments establish the epistemic conditions necessary for juridical systems to operate prior to the manifestation of irreversible change.

Within the c-ECO framework, this scientific capacity is translated into juridically operative form through Systemic Proof, defined as certified, auditable, and continuously updated technical evidence derived from monitored system variables. Legal effects are not grounded in speculative prediction but in the certification of system conditions through institutional mechanisms that ensure traceability, verification, and epistemic integrity (Biermann et al., 2012).

Systemic Proof operates through the Threshold Function Protocol (TFP), which integrates four certified variables—Position (P), Velocity (ΔV), Uncertainty (σ), and Reversibility Liquidity (Lr)—to determine the system's condition relative to the Safe Operating Space (SOS). Position measures proximity to critical thresholds, Velocity captures the direction and rate of change, Uncertainty reflects the degree of epistemic confidence in the available data, and Reversibility Liquidity indicates the system's remaining capacity to absorb disturbances and recover.

Within a given trajectory, the Threshold Function Protocol translates these variables into classifications that determine juridical consequences. When certified conditions indicate convergence toward irreversibility, legal effects arise ex lege, prior to the materialization of harm. Intervention is thus anchored in the depletion of Reversibility Liquidity rather than in the occurrence of damage.

This transformation redefines the relationship between legality and time. Legal validity ceases to function as a static attribute determined at the moment of formation and becomes a dynamic condition contingent upon continuous systemic compatibility. The enforceability and continuity of obligations are no longer guaranteed by formal compliance alone but are conditioned upon the preservation of reversibility within the Safe Operating Space.

Predictive legality, however, raises significant concerns regarding legal certainty, due process, and the distribution of authority between juridical and technical domains. Conditioning legal effects upon dynamic system variables may appear to destabilize the predictability of legal relations. Similarly, reliance on technical data and automated classification mechanisms may be perceived as shifting authority away from traditional adjudicative institutions.

These concerns are neither incidental nor trivial. They reflect structural tensions inherent in any attempt to align juridical systems with non-linear, data-intensive, and continuously evolving physical processes.

The c-ECO framework addresses these tensions through a reconfiguration rather than a rejection of established legal principles. Legal certainty is preserved through the use of predefined and publicly auditable thresholds, ensuring that the conditions triggering juridical effects are transparent and predictable. Due process is maintained through institutional arrangements that separate measurement, verification, and adjudication, preventing the concentration of authority within a single domain. Technical evidence acquires legal relevance only when certified through structured verification processes, ensuring its admissibility and contestability.

Furthermore, juridical responses are structured in graduated form, allowing for proportionate intervention based on the degree of systemic deviation. Continuous monitoring mechanisms ensure that classifications remain subject to revision in light of updated data, while auditability guarantees that all determinations can be traced, reviewed, and contested within established procedural frameworks (Vinuales, 2020).

Within this architecture, predictive legality does not eliminate juridical discretion but repositions it. Judicial and institutional review are no longer centered on reconstructing causal chains or reassessing probabilistic risks, but on verifying the integrity of the data, the correctness of the classification, and the proper functioning of the underlying procedural structures.

The emergence of predictive legality thus marks a decisive transformation in the logic of law. Law ceases to operate primarily as a system that reacts to damage or anticipates risk, and becomes instead a system that governs trajectories through the continuous verification of systemic conditions. Juridical relevance is no longer anchored in events or probabilities but in the dynamic evolution of systems relative to their operational limits.

This transformation is not merely methodological but structural. It reflects the incorporation of Earth system constraints into the very conditions of juridical validity, establishing a regime in which the preservation of reversibility and systemic stability becomes the primary criterion for legal operability.

In this sense, predictive legality does not represent an incremental development within existing doctrines. It constitutes a shift in the temporal and ontological foundations of law itself, positioning juridical systems to operate within, rather than after, the dynamics of the systems they seek to govern.

Chapter 4 Ontological Foundations of Systemic Governance: Material Primacy and the Reconceptualization of Juridical Validity

The doctrinal developments examined in the preceding chapters—namely the displacement of juridical temporality, the erosion of linear causality, and the emergence of predictive legality—culminate in a fundamental transformation concerning the ontological foundations of law itself. This shift transcends mere operational adjustments, affecting the very conditions under which law is understood to exist in relation to the material systems it purports to govern.

Historically, classical legal theory, particularly within its positivist traditions, maintained a rigorous distinction between legal validity and material reality. As articulated in the foundational works of Kelsen (1967) and Hart (1994), legal norms derive their validity from formal, self-referential sources—such as legislation, precedent, or institutional recognition—rather than from the physical conditions of their application. Within this framework, law functions as a relatively autonomous system, generating its own internal criteria of validity independently of external biophysical constraints.

This conception provided the structural stability necessary for modern industrial societies. However, it rested upon an implicit assumption: that the material environment within which law operates would remain sufficiently stable, predictable, and ultimately subordinate to human regulation.

The emergence of Earth system constraints in the Anthropocene fundamentally destabilizes this assumption. Scientific advances have demonstrated that human activity operates within non-negotiable biophysical limits. The concept of planetary boundaries establishes the existence of a Safe Operating Space within which systemic stability may be maintained, beyond which non-linear and potentially irreversible transformations may occur (Rockström et al., 2009; Steffen et al., 2015). These limits are not external variables subject to normative negotiation; they constitute the material conditions of possibility for social, economic, and institutional systems.

Within this context, the autonomy of law becomes materially bounded. Legal norms may continue to exist in a formal sense, but their operability becomes contingent upon their compatibility with underlying physical systems. Where legal arrangements project trajectories that exceed systemic thresholds, a structural contradiction emerges: the execution of legally valid acts contributes to the erosion of the very conditions required for their continued existence. In this respect, the c-ECO framework converges with Earth System Law scholarship, which argues that legal order can no longer be conceived as indifferent to the Earth system conditions that sustain it (Kotzé and Kim, 2019).

Law, therefore, can no longer be conceived as an external force acting upon reality from a position of autonomy. It must instead be understood as structurally embedded within the material systems that condition its operability.

This shift necessitates a transition from formal compliance to material compatibility as the operative benchmark of legality. Within the c-ECO framework, this transformation is articulated through the principle of material primacy. The Earth system is not treated as an object of regulation, but as a constitutive condition of juridical operability. Legal relations, obligations, and institutional arrangements retain formal validity, but their enforceability and continuity become conditioned upon systemic compatibility.

This introduces a dynamic conception of juridical validity. Under classical doctrine, validity is determined at the moment of formation and presumed to persist unless formally annulled. Within a system governed by dynamic constraints, this static conception becomes insufficient. Legal relations unfold over time and interact with evolving system conditions. As trajectories develop, a legal act that was initially valid may become materially non-viable, not through violation of legal norms, but through incompatibility with the preservation of reversibility and systemic stability. This movement away from static validity toward materially conditioned operability also resonates with systems-theoretical accounts of law that emphasize the structural coupling of normative and social orders (Luhmann, 2004).

This condition is more precisely understood as conditional subsistence. Legal relations do not simply exist; they subsist conditionally, dependent upon the maintenance of compatibility with the Safe Operating Space and the preservation of Reversibility Liquidity. Where trajectories indicate convergence toward irreversibility, the enforceability of obligations is suspended, and the continuity of legal relations is interrupted. This interruption arises not from breach or fault, but from the material conditions under which execution becomes incompatible with system stability. In this sense, c-ECO extends beyond conventional precaution by repositioning juridical intervention at the point where reversibility itself becomes endangered rather than at the point where harm becomes compensable (Gardiner, 2011; Trouwborst, 2002).

This reconfiguration also transforms the relationship between law and knowledge. Traditional evidentiary regimes—predominantly retrospective, interpretative, and probabilistic—are insufficient for governing dynamic systems. Within the c-ECO framework, evidentiary structures are reoriented toward Systemic Proof, defined as certified, auditable, and continuously updated technical evidence derived from monitored system conditions. Legal relevance is grounded not in reconstructed narratives or probabilistic projections, but in the real-time verification of system states. This evidentiary transition reflects broader transformations in global governance, where monitoring, scientific assessment, and institutionalized knowledge increasingly shape legal and regulatory coordination (Biermann et al., 2012; Reid et al., 2010).

This evidentiary regime is operationalized through institutional mechanisms that ensure traceability, verification, and epistemic integrity, including structured certification processes and distributed validation functions. Uncertainty is not eliminated but internalized as a parameter within system analysis and treated asymmetrically in favor of preserving the remaining capacity for reversibility. This asymmetrical treatment of uncertainty places the framework within the broader trajectory of precautionary reasoning, while also moving beyond it by converting uncertainty into a trigger for ongoing systemic verification rather than merely a justification for one-off intervention (Sandin, 1999; Wiener and Rogers, 2002).

The ontological implications of this transformation are profound. Law ceases to function primarily as a system for resolving disputes or assigning responsibility after the fact. It becomes a system for maintaining the conditions under which social, economic, and institutional relations remain possible. Its primary objective shifts from retrospective correction to the preservation of future viability. This shift aligns with broader arguments that legal and governance systems in the Anthropocene must be reoriented toward safeguarding the functional integrity of Earth system processes rather than merely allocating ex post responsibility (Bosselmann, 2017; Kotzé, 2014).

This transformation does not abolish the autonomy of law, but redefines it. The classical separation between the normative "ought" (Sollen) and the empirical "is" (Sein), central to positivist legal theory, is not eliminated but reconfigured. In the Anthropocene, the normative domain remains distinct, but its operability becomes materially bounded by biophysical constraints. Legal autonomy persists, but it is no longer the autonomy of a system detached from its environment; it is the autonomy of a system that survives through its continuous calibration to non-linear system dynamics.

Within this reconfigured framework, juridical form becomes functionally internalized within the systems it governs. Legal validity becomes a function of compatibility, enforceability becomes contingent upon reversibility, and juridical intervention is structured around the preservation of systemic stability. The distinction between legal fact and biophysical condition is not dissolved, but integrated within a unified evidentiary architecture in which system states acquire direct juridical relevance through certified verification processes.

This marks the completion of the transformation initiated in the preceding chapters. From retrospective adjudication to predictive legality, and from causality to trajectory, the evolution of juridical reasoning culminates in a redefinition of its ontological foundations. Law no longer operates as a system that reacts to a reality already degraded, nor merely anticipates future harm. It becomes a system that actively maintains the conditions that render reality governable.

In this context, the primary objective of the juridical order is no longer the attribution of liability, but the preservation of the Safe Operating Space, ensuring that the material foundations of juridical existence remain viable for future institutional and social relations.

Interpretative Framework and Systemic Legal Rationale

Chapters 5–10
[CECO-PART-II-OV]

Systemic Interpretation and the Architecture of Legal Reasoning

The interpretative framework governing the c-ECO regime establishes principles for the construction, application, and evolution of its normative provisions. Unlike conventional legal instruments that prioritize textual fixity and original intent, the c-ECO framework adopts a systemic, purposive, and evolutive approach to interpretation, guided by the overarching objective of preserving Earth System integrity (Craig and de Búrca, 2020; Young, 2011).

Chapter 5 addresses the purpose and scope of the regime, establishing its applicability to contracts, assets, operations, and infrastructures whose execution interferes with critical planetary boundaries. Chapter 6 develops the concept of Trajectory Illegality and the Predictive Legal Nexus, providing the doctrinal architecture for anticipatory governance. Chapter 7 examines the Safe Operating Space as a legally binding constraint, while Chapter 8 traces the transition from retrospective liability to ex ante governance (Peel, 2005).

Chapters 9 and 10 complete this Part by establishing the centrality of Systemic Proof and the structural legal instruments of anticipatory protection. Throughout, the interpretative approach maintains fidelity to the precautionary principle, the asymmetry of uncertainty, and the material primacy of biophysical limits (Trouwborst, 2002; Wiener and Rogers, 2012).

Structural Principles
III

Structural Principles of the c-ECO Regime

Chapters 11–15
[CECO-PART-III-OV]

Supremacy, Precaution, and Pre-Threshold Governance

The structural principles articulated in Chapters 11 through 15 constitute the normative backbone of the c-ECO regime, establishing hierarchies of value, decision-rules under uncertainty, and the temporal orientation of legal intervention. These principles operate as meta-norms, conditioning the interpretation and application of all other provisions within the Statute (Alexy, 2002; Dworkin, 1986).

Chapter 11 establishes the Supremacy of the Safe Operating Space, asserting that no legal effect may be recognized for acts or arrangements whose execution presupposes or entails violation of monitored systemic limits. This principle of biophysical supremacy represents a radical departure from anthropocentric legal frameworks, subordinating human preferences to material constraints (Bosselmann, 2017; Taylor, 1998).

Chapter 12 articulates the In Dubio Pro Systema principle and the treatment of technical thresholds. Where material uncertainty exists regarding risk trajectories, impact reversibility, or systemic integrity, legal decision-making shall prioritize protection of the system. This asymmetric treatment of uncertainty—where doubt contracts rather than expands operational space—operationalizes the precautionary principle within legal procedure (Sandin, 1999; Steele, 2006).

Chapter 13 affirms the Principle of Physical Primacy and the Non-Disposability of Collapse Risk, establishing that the risk of systemic collapse constitutes a non-disposable legal interest that cannot be waived, settled, or normalized through party consent or administrative authorization. Chapter 14 develops the principle of Ex-Ante Governance and Systemic Perclusion, while Chapter 15 establishes the Pre-Threshold Principle, recognizing juridical relevance before harm materialization (Gardiner, 2006; Hiskes, 2009).

Systemic Paradigm
IV

The Systemic Legal Paradigm and Functional Integrity

Chapters 16–18
[CECO-PART-IV-OV]

Functional Integrity and Conditional Validity

The Systemic Legal Paradigm, developed in Chapters 16 through 18, reconceptualizes legal validity as a dynamic, conditional, and materially embedded property rather than a static, formal attribute. This paradigm shift has profound implications for contract law, property rights, and the theory of legal obligations (Teubner, 1983; Luhmann, 2004).

Chapter 16 establishes Functional Integrity as a Condition of Legal Validity. Under this principle, the validity, efficacy, and enforceability of acts, contracts, and operations are determined by their functional integrity within the Earth System. Legal validity becomes a continuous legal condition, subject to ongoing verification of systemic compatibility, rather than a static attribute derived solely from formal compliance at the time of formation (Kotzé, 2019).

Chapter 17 articulates the Rule of Systemic Compatibility, whereby formal lawfulness, regulatory authorization, or administrative compliance is insufficient to ensure the subsistence or enforceability of legal acts. Systemic non-compliance—demonstrated through certified technical evidence of trajectory incompatibility—authorizes ex lege the immediate application of Ex-Ante Unenforceability and Systemic Perclusion, irrespective of fault, intent, or prior materialization of physical harm (Vinuales, 2020; Kim and Bosselmann, 2013).

Chapter 18 concludes this Part by establishing the Conditional Subsistence of Legal Bonds. The subsistence, enforceability, and continuity of legal relationships subject to the c-ECO regime are strictly conditioned upon predictive technical verification of Trajectory Stability and Material Reversibility. The verified loss of material reversibility, or trajectory deviation exceeding established probability thresholds, constitutes Systemic Default ex lege, resulting in immediate suspension of contractual rights and priority of restorative and curatorial duties (Biermann et al., 2012; Galaz et al., 2012).
