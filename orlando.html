Introduction

The Atlas Case

At 3:47 a.m. on March 14, 2028, a monitoring system installed in the Rio Doce basin issued an alert.

It was not a flood alert, not a dam breach alert, and not any other event that conventional systems are built to recognize. It was an alert of another order: a trajectory alert. The system had identified that a process of ecosystem collapse was already underway, even though no visible physical sign had yet appeared.

Had such a system existed in 2015, the Mariana disaster might not have been merely answered after the fact, as it inevitably was, but interrupted before becoming irreversible.

The Atlas case is hypothetical. Its elements are not. The technological architecture, the contractual logic, and the institutional implications already exist.

Atlas Consortium appeared exemplary: permits approved, environmental studies validated, financing secured, billions in international capital mobilized. Nothing seemed irregular. Nothing seemed illegal. Everything was formally compliant.

And yet the problem was already there — not in an event, but in the trajectory.

Nothing had happened yet. The water was still potable. Communities had not yet been affected. There was no crisis, no litigation, no headline.

And yet, the contract stopped.

It stopped because it contained a clause connecting the dynamics of the biophysical system to contractual enforceability itself. Once the system’s trajectory became incompatible with stability, the obligation ceased to be enforceable — not as sanction, not as breach, but as a legal response to reality.

First Pillar

Systemic Risk

The Atlas case matters because it reveals that the most consequential risk today is often not visible risk, nor already materialized damage, but systemic risk: the risk that emerges from interdependence, from non-linearity, and from the possibility of irreversibility.

Law has historically been structured around acts. It examines isolated conduct, discrete decisions, identifiable events. But complex systems do not fail that way. They fail through accumulation, through compounding interactions, through sequences of individually lawful decisions that together produce a destabilizing direction.

Systemic risks do not present themselves as events. They manifest as trajectories.

Second Pillar

Expanded Due Diligence

If the relevant risk is trajectory, then traditional due diligence is no longer enough. Classical diligence verifies compliance: whether permits were obtained, whether formal requirements were met, whether regulations were followed.

But it leaves unanswered the more consequential question: what happens if all of those lawful decisions continue over time? Expanded due diligence begins precisely there. It does not ask only whether a project is lawful today. It asks whether its continuation remains compatible with systemic stability over time.

The shift is fundamental. The analysis moves from conformity to compatibility.

Third Pillar

Pre-Threshold Governance

01

Before damage

Intervention is displaced from aftermath to trajectory, from reaction to anticipatory legal structuring.

02

Before irreversibility

The relevant window is the period in which recalibration remains materially possible.

03

Before collapse becomes fate

The legal problem is no longer damage alone, but the exhaustion of reversibility itself.

Pre-threshold governance does not mean generic prevention. It does not call for intervention against every possible risk. It operates when there is sufficient evidence that the continuation of a trajectory will compromise the system’s remaining capacity for reversal.

This is where law is asked to do something unusual: to decide not only on what has already happened, but on what is already underway.

The problem was never the absence of information. Data exists. Models exist. The capacity to identify critical trajectories already exists.

What has historically been missing, at least in operational legal form, is the ability to transform that information into legal consequence before damage occurs.

The central issue, then, is no longer whether we can see. It is what we do with what has been seen, and when that knowledge begins to alter obligations, suspend contracts, or redistribute responsibilities.

Seeing the invisible is not only a matter of technology or mathematics.
It is a legal decision.
