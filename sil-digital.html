Part I

Domain Intelligence Profile

Digital Technology & Data Infrastructure is treated as a systemic substrate: the physical and virtual layer through which finance, healthcare, energy systems, governance, logistics, emergency services, and knowledge systems maintain operational continuity.

Domain Definition

The domain includes fiber optic cables, data centers, satellite constellations, radio-frequency spectrum, cloud computing platforms, DNS infrastructure, IXPs, terrestrial networks, submarine cable landing stations, and the power and cooling systems that sustain computational continuity.

Fiber Cloud GNSS IXPs

Systemic Function

Digital infrastructure acts as the central nervous system of contemporary economic execution. Its failure can propagate rapidly into payment systems, hospital operations, emergency communications, energy dispatch, logistics coordination, public administration, and market functioning.

Cross-domain substrate Cascade risk
Dense Content — Domain Overview

The Digital Technology & Data Infrastructure domain consists of the interconnected physical, orbital, electromagnetic, computational, and governance systems that process, store, route, secure, and transmit information. It includes terrestrial fiber networks, submarine cable systems, mobile networks, 5G and future 6G systems, hyperscale data centers, cloud platforms, satellite constellations, GNSS timing infrastructure, DNS and routing systems, internet exchange points, spectrum allocation regimes, cybersecurity response systems, and computational infrastructures supporting artificial intelligence.

Its systemic relevance derives from dependency concentration. Financial markets depend on digital timing, payment rails, and cloud-hosted infrastructure. Health systems depend on electronic records, telemedicine, diagnostic devices, supply chains, and emergency communications. Energy systems depend on digital control, telemetry, grid dispatch, and cyber-physical monitoring. Public administration increasingly depends on digital identity, digital taxation, online service delivery, and public alerting systems.

The domain is therefore not merely a sector. It is a systemic transmission layer through which shocks in energy, space, water, cybersecurity, climate, logistics, and governance can propagate into other domains. A digital failure may begin as a software bug, a cable cut, a routing instability, a power interruption, or a cooling event, but it may become systemic when it disables the ability of other domains to observe, coordinate, transact, respond, or recover.

Dense Content — Historical Evolution and ΔV Calibration Material

Historical digital infrastructure disruptions provide calibration material for trajectory analysis. The 1859 Carrington Event remains a benchmark for geomagnetic exposure. The 1989 Quebec blackout demonstrated how geomagnetically induced currents can saturate high-voltage transformers and cause rapid grid failure. The 2006 Hengchun earthquake demonstrated geographical concentration risk when multiple submarine cables were severed simultaneously. The 2010 Stuxnet incident demonstrated cyber-to-physical cascade dynamics.

Recent events show increasing coupling among digital systems, power systems, climate stress, and geopolitical risk. The 2022 Tonga eruption exposed the fragility of single-cable island connectivity. The 2022 Google Cloud London cooling incident linked heat stress to cloud availability. The 2024 Red Sea cable cuts demonstrated geopolitical exposure of intercontinental data routes. The 2025 Spain/Portugal blackout illustrates Energy → Digital cascade risk, where grid instability produced major telecommunications and internet traffic degradation.

These events are not only historical cases. Under the TFP they function as calibration evidence for ΔV: the velocity of deterioration, the speed of cascade propagation, the timing of recovery, and the institutional latency between detection and response.

Primary Risk Logic

Primary risks include fiber cuts, submarine cable rupture, hyperscale data center outage, GNSS spoofing, grid power failure, cooling failure, geomagnetic storms, Kessler Syndrome, BGP hijacking, software supply chain compromise, and certificate authority collapse.

Liability Cascade Architecture

Liability in this domain is not confined to the initial failure point. A cable owner, cloud provider, data center operator, software vendor, or satellite operator may trigger cascading loss in finance, healthcare, logistics, energy, or public administration.

  • Primary liability: infrastructure operator or digital service provider.
  • Secondary liability: dependent sectors that fail to maintain fallback capacity.
  • Tertiary liability: insurance and reinsurance structures exposed to systemic digital loss.
  • c-ECO layer: Restoration Fund or pre-contracted recovery execution where private insurance is insufficient.
Data Sovereignty and Reversibility

Legal fragmentation can reduce Reversibility Liquidity. If a cloud workload cannot move across borders during crisis because of conflicting data localization, privacy, security, or government-access regimes, the technical ability to fail over may exist while legal reversibility is effectively zero.

TFP implication: cross-border failover capacity must be evaluated as both a technical and legal Lr component. A redundant system that cannot lawfully activate during crisis should not be treated as fully reversible.

Uncertainty (σ) Intelligence

Uncertainty enters the digital domain through proprietary monitoring systems, incomplete outage reporting, invisible dependencies, closed cloud architectures, non-public routing policies, cybersecurity underreporting, space debris model uncertainty, and jurisdictional non-disclosure. In c-ECO logic, uncertainty does not enlarge the permission to continue. It contracts the safe margin.

  • Measurement uncertainty: sensor, telemetry, and monitoring limits.
  • Model uncertainty: space weather, debris cascade, cyber exploit probability, and cloud dependency modeling.
  • Governance uncertainty: reporting delays, political opacity, private provider opacity, and cross-border legal conflict.
  • Operational uncertainty: whether recovery capacity can actually be mobilized under simultaneous stress.
Reversibility Liquidity (Lr) Components

Financial and Contractual Lr

  • Disaster recovery budget allocation.
  • Cyber insurance limits and exclusions.
  • Contingency contracts with cable repair firms.
  • Cloud failover contracts.
  • Performance-backed restoration agreements.

Technical and Operational Lr

  • Dark fiber and alternative routing capacity.
  • IXP spare bandwidth.
  • Mobile backup power duration.
  • Manual fallback procedures.
  • Satellite backup bandwidth.
  • Truck-roll and fiber repair capacity.
  • Alternative cooling water sources.
