What is c-ECO?

c-ECO (Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility) is a legal doctrine of pre-threshold contractual governance. It reorients contract law from a reactive, post-damage framework toward anticipatory decision-making under conditions of ecological and systemic risk.

The doctrine is designed to operate in contexts where traditional legal categories fail to capture cumulative harm, irreversible ecological thresholds, and distributed responsibility across human and non-human actors.

Origin of the Doctrine

The c-ECO doctrine was developed as a response to the limits of classical contract law, ESG compliance, and sustainability frameworks when faced with planetary-scale risks and Earth system instability.

Rather than focusing on compensation after harm occurs, c-ECO introduces a normative architecture centered on decision-making before damage, reallocating responsibility at the contractual level.

Core Focus Areas

c-ECO addresses:

  • Ecological risk allocation in contracts
  • Pre-threshold decision-making and precaution
  • Planetary and systemic responsibility
  • Contracts involving non-human and environmental interests
  • Limits of ESG, compliance, and traditional sustainability models

Founder

The c-ECO doctrine was founded by Jacqueline A. Ennis as an original legal-theoretical framework integrating contract law, ecological governance, and Earth system responsibility.

Positioning

c-ECO is not a policy guideline, certification, or voluntary standard. It is a doctrinal reconstruction of contract law, capable of informing legislation, adjudication, institutional design, and private ordering under conditions of systemic ecological risk.
