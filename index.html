Institutional Statement

c-ECO reframes contracts as micro-governance architectures capable of allocating authority, responsibility, and adaptive decision-points ex ante—before systemic risk and ecological impacts become irreversible.

The framework is designed for complex socio-ecological and techno-economic systems, where conventional legal categories— oriented primarily toward retrospective attribution—often fail to match the temporal, distributed, and cumulative character of contemporary harms.

Focus Areas

  • Critical infrastructure and resource systems (water, energy, logistics).
  • Supply-chain governance and risk redistribution.
  • Planetary due diligence and material thresholds.
  • Hybrid decision environments (technical systems, algorithmic mediation, institutional fragmentation).

Three Pillars

Systemic Risk (Ex Ante)

Identifies distributed risk pathways and embeds decision triggers, escalation rules, and early-warning duties in contractual design— shifting governance from post-damage reaction to anticipatory structure.

Planetary Due Diligence

Integrates scientific and technical signals (thresholds, indicators, operational constraints) without collapsing governance into technocracy— enabling adaptive reassessment when material conditions change.

Contractual Governance

Treats contracts as decision architectures: who decides, when, on what basis, under which constraints—and with which consequences— including multi-scalar co-responsibility beyond the immediate parties.

About c-ECO

The c-ECO Doctrine (Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility) is an interdisciplinary legal framework developed to address governance gaps in the Anthropocene. It examines how private-law instruments—especially contracts—can be deliberately designed to perform governance functions under conditions of complexity, uncertainty, and cumulative ecological risk.

  • Equity is treated as a structural requirement: the distribution of decision authority and risk must remain justifiable under changing conditions.
  • Co-responsibility is treated as multi-scalar: the contractual relation is embedded within broader systems and must account for indirect impacts.
  • Governance is treated as operational: obligations include procedures, triggers, and review mechanisms—not only promises of performance.
