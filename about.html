Who We Are

From the Amazon
to Systemic Governance.

c-ECO was not born in a library. It was born from 22 years of running businesses in Belém do Pará — where contracts failed, supply chains collapsed, and systemic risk was not a concept but a daily reality. The framework emerged when that lived experience met formal legal training and Earth System Law.

Origin

The Story Behind the Framework

1993 — 2015
Entrepreneur in the Amazon
Jacqueline A. Ennis built and operated two businesses in Belém do Pará: the African Bar (1993–2005) and the Palafita Bar (2005–2015). For 22 years, she managed contracts, supply chains, and operations in one of Brazil's most complex logistical and environmental contexts. She learned firsthand how contracts break when systemic conditions shift — how a supplier's default is rarely about the supplier, but about the river, the road, the rain, or the regulatory vacuum.
2016 — 2021
From Brazil to Boston
In 2016, Jacqueline moved to the United States. She completed Paralegal Studies at Boston University (2021), working simultaneously as Contract Administrator at Bluebird Network — where she saw the same patterns: contracts designed for stability that become traps when external conditions change. The legal instruments she managed had force majeure clauses, but no language for gradual systemic decay.
2021 — 2023
The LL.M. and the Recognition-Action Gap
During her LL.M. in Business Law at Washington University in St. Louis (2021–2023), Jacqueline formalized what she had observed for decades. She identified a structural gap in contract and governance law: legal systems recognize harm only after it materializes. There is no juridical category for pre-threshold responsibility — the duty to act when systemic trajectory is detectably constrained but before formal breach occurs. This became the core of the c-ECO Doctrine.
2023 — 2024
COP30 and the Birth of the Doctrine
When Belém was selected to host COP30, Jacqueline began developing a project to present at the conference. Studying contractual architectures in Amazonian environmental cases — hydrovia projects, mining concessions, land tenure conflicts — she saw the same pattern repeated: contracts that lock in irreversible commitments before environmental and social constraints are integrated. The c-ECO framework emerged as a direct response: a governance architecture designed to preserve decision reversibility before the threshold of no return.
2024 — 2026
From Doctrine to System
The c-ECO Statute (232 articles), the TFP Operational Manual (153 pages), and two applied cases — Yemen Energy Contracts and Amazon Tapajós Infrastructure — transformed lived experience into a replicable methodology. The Johann Christian Hasse Foundation was established to house the institutional development. A partnership with Instituto Silvio Meira was initiated for Brazil-based operational articulation. The Fellowship and Pilot programs were designed to train researchers and test the framework with live organizational cases.
I didn't invent systemic risk. I survived it for 22 years in the Amazon. What I invented was a language to describe what I was seeing — and a framework to act on it before the contracts break.
— Jacqueline A. Ennis, Founder, c-ECO Systemic Governance Initiative
Where We Actually Are

We are not a consulting firm with 500 employees. We are not a multilateral institution with field offices. We are a research-driven initiative founded by someone who spent two decades running businesses in one of the world's most complex systemic environments, then spent a decade formalizing what she learned into law.

What we have: A rigorous framework (TFP/TDR/Safe Mode), two published case applications, a 232-article governance statute, a 153-page operational manual, and a founder with 22 years of lived experience in systemic risk environments plus formal legal training at Boston University and WashU Law.

What we don't have yet: A live implementation with a real organization, a certified Data Custodian network, or a track record of enterprise deployment.

What we need: Organizations willing to let us apply the framework to one concrete case — a contract, an operation, a risk system — under supervised conditions, with full transparency about our stage of development.

What We Offer — Honestly

For Researchers & Fellows

The c-ECO Fellowship is a selective applied program (2 positions per cycle) where you work on a real or proposed case under direct supervision. You learn the framework by using it. You do not get a degree. You may get recognition and, in exceptional cases, a license to use c-ECO in future work.

View Current Call →

For Organizations

The c-ECO Pilot Program is not a standard consulting engagement. It is a supervised application of the framework to one case within your organization — a contract, an operation, a risk system. You get a structured diagnostic, not a finished solution. We get validation. The price is set per case, not per hour.

For Institutions & Multilaterals

We are in early conversations with organizations like LRQA (supply chain risk) and Polaris Corporate Risk (due diligence) about how c-ECO could complement their services. No partnerships are finalized. If you represent an institution interested in exploring integration, we are open to structured conversations — but we will not claim capabilities we do not yet have.
