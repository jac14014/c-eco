     [CLASS B — Estimated]

     [CLASS A — Legal statutes]

   θ_k ~ Beta(α_k, β_k)  for probability-scale parameters
   θ_k ~ Gamma(α_k, β_k) for rate-scale parameters
   θ_k ~ Normal(µ_k, σ²_k) for continuous-scale parameters
Where hyperparameters (α_k, β_k, µ_k, σ²_k) are derived from:

   D_t | θ_k ~ f(D_t | θ_k, φ_k)

   π(θ_k | D_{1:t}) ∝ π(θ_k | D_{1:t-1}) × f(D_t | θ_k, φ_k)
Or in log-form for computational stability:
   log π(θ_k | D_{1:t}) = log π(θ_k | D_{1:t-1}) + log f(D_t | θ_k, φ_k) + const
This enables:

   • r: Risk-adjusted discount rate
   • L(τ): Avoided loss value at activation time τ
   • C(τ): Activation cost at time τ
   • F_t: Information filtration at time t (all available signals)

   V(X×, t) = L(X*) - C(X*)                               (value matching)
   ∂V/∂X (X*, t) = L'(X*) - C'(X*)                       (smooth pasting)
Where µ(X,t) and σ²(X,t) are the drift and volatility of the damage 
process, estimated from:
   • Historical damage trajectory analysis [CLASS A — Institutional data]

4.1.1 Out-of-Sample Testing

4.2 UNCERTAINTY QUANTIFICATION
4.2.1 Aleatoric Uncertainty (Irreducible)
Quantified through:
   • Ensemble spread (variance across model predictions)
   • Posterior predictive distribution variance
   • Bootstrap resampling of historical events
   σ²_aleatoric = Var_{model}[E_{data}[Y | X, θ]] + E_{model}[Var_{data}[Y | X, θ]]
4.2.2 Epistemic Uncertainty (Reducible)
Quantified through:
   • Bayesian posterior variance over model parameters
   • Sensitivity analysis (Sobol indices, Morris screening)
   • Model structural uncertainty (multi-model ensemble)
   σ²_epistemic = Var_{model}[E_{data}[Y | X, θ]]
4.2.3 Total Uncertainty Decomposition
   σ²_total = σ²_aleatoric + σ²_epistemic + 2 × Cov(aleatoric, epistemic)
Decision rule: Activation confidence requires σ²_total < τ_uncertainty
[CLASS B — Uncertainty decomposition framework; no validated uncertainty 
quantification for c-ECO deployments]
4.3 EXTREME VALUE THEORY — TAIL RISK MODELING

For low-frequency, high-severity events (catastrophic tail risk):
   Generalized Pareto Distribution (GPD) for exceedances over threshold u:
   
   P(X - u > x | X > u) = (1 + ξx/σ)^{-1/ξ}   for ξ ≠ 0
   
   Where:
   • ξ: Shape parameter (tail heaviness)
   • σ: Scale parameter
   • u: Threshold (typically 95th percentile of historical data)
Parameter estimation:
   • Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) with profile likelihood for ξ
   • Bayesian estimation with informative priors from physical constraints
   • Penalized likelihood for small samples (L-moments, Bayesian shrinkage)
Return level estimation:
   
   x_m = u + (σ/ξ) × [(m × n_u/N × ζ_u)^{ξ} - 1]
   
   Where:
   • x_m: m-year return level
   • n_u: Number of exceedances over threshold u
   • N: Total number of observations
   • ζ_u: Probability of exceeding threshold u

9. LIMITATIONS AND ASSUMPTIONS
═════════════════════════════════════════════════
══════════════════════════════
9.1 MATHEMATICAL LIMITATIONS
• Bayesian priors require expert elicitation or historical data that may 
  not exist for novel risk contexts
• State-space models assume linearity and Gaussian noise that may not 
  hold for catastrophic tail events
• Real options valuation requires continuous-time stochastic processes 
  that are approximations of discrete reality
• Extreme value theory assumes asymptotic convergence that may not be 
  achieved with limited historical samples
9.2 DATA LIMITATIONS
• Sensor networks may fail during the events they are designed to detect 
  (survivorship bias)
• Satellite data has temporal and spatial resolution constraints
• Regulatory data may have reporting lags of months or years
• Market data may not reflect emerging risks (information asymmetry)
9.3 OPERATIONAL LIMITATIONS
• Automated activation may face legal challenges (liability, due process)
• Stakeholder coordination requires trust and pre-established protocols
• False activation costs may exceed prevention benefits in low-probability 
  scenarios
• Cascade dynamics may be faster than containment response times
9.4 EPISTEMIC LIMITATIONS

• All models are wrong; some are useful (Box, 1976)
• Unknown unknowns (black swan events) cannot be modeled
• Model drift requires continuous recalibration
• Stakeholder preferences may change faster than model updates
