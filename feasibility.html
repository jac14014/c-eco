Evidence Governance Framework
Classification System

All claims, estimates, and projections in this document are classified according to the c-ECO Evidence Governance Framework, which distinguishes three epistemic classes.

CLASS A — Externally Benchmarked / Empirically Verifiable
Definition

Data, benchmarks, or findings independently verifiable by third parties through publicly available sources, audited records, or reproducible methodologies.

Use: Due diligence, regulatory filings, contractual representations, investor disclosures.

Transition: A→B or A→C only upon discovery of methodological error or obsolescence. Reclassification requires documented review.

CLASS B — Modeled / Strategic / Hypothetical
Definition

Estimates, projections, scenarios, or inferences derived from analytical models, strategic assumptions, or comparative reasoning. Not empirically validated for the specific c-ECO application.

Use: Internal planning, fundraising with appropriate disclaimers, strategic positioning, research agendas.

Transition Protocol B→A: Requires independent third-party validation, statistical significance, reproducible methodology, and audit trail.

Transition Protocol B→C: Requires identification of fundamental structural flaws in underlying assumptions.

CLASS C — Conceptually Coherent / Not Operationally Validated
Definition

Architectural concepts, theoretical frameworks, or design principles demonstrating internal logical consistency and alignment with established systems, but lacking operational implementation or empirical testing.

Use: R&D roadmaps, vision documents, architectural design, partnership discussions, academic publication.

Transition Protocol C→B: Requires prototyping, simulation, or limited-scope pilot with documented assumptions and known limitations.

Transition Protocol C→A: Requires full operational deployment with independent measurement and control group.

XIII. Technical Architecture Annex
Trigger Function Protocol — Mathematical Methodology
[CLASS C — Conceptual Architecture; CLASS B — Mathematical Modeling Framework]

This Technical Architecture Annex presents the conceptual mathematical framework, threshold derivation methodology, signal architecture design, and uncertainty treatment protocols for the c-ECO Trigger Function Protocol and associated predictive governance components.

The architecture remains an architectural prototype under development. No operational deployment has been validated. Mathematical frameworks are theoretical constructs requiring empirical calibration, field testing, and independent audit before reclassification to CLASS B or CLASS A.

A. Trigger Function Protocol — Conceptual Architecture

The Trigger Function Protocol is a conceptual governance activation mechanism that executes pre-defined legal and operational protocols upon predictive threshold breach. It operates as a decision-theoretic framework translating multi-dimensional risk signals into actionable governance interventions before irreversible systemic degradation.

Input Layer: IoT sensors, satellite imagery, weather feeds, regulatory data, market data.

Processing Layer: signal fusion, threshold calibration, Bayesian inference, ensemble modeling, real options valuation, uncertainty quantification.

Output Layer: legal pre-emption, operational halt, reserve mobilization, stakeholder alert, insurance trigger, governance activation.

B. Threshold Derivation — Bayesian Inference Model

The TFP threshold derivation employs a Bayesian hierarchical model integrating prior domain knowledge with real-time observational data to produce dynamically calibrated activation thresholds.

For each risk dimension — environmental, operational, financial, regulatory, and reputational — prior distributions encode historical baseline risk parameters, sector-specific vulnerability profiles, and regulatory compliance boundaries.

Prior Construction: historical event frequency, expert elicitation, physical process modeling, jurisdictional compliance boundaries.

Likelihood Function: observational data modeled with measurement error, sensor calibration drift, data latency, and temporal autocorrelation.

Posterior Updating: sequential Bayesian updating allows threshold adaptation as new data arrives.

Decision Boundary: activation thresholds are derived under asymmetric loss functions balancing false negatives and false positives.

The TFP integrates heterogeneous signals with varying reliability, latency, and predictive horizon. Signal categories include physical sensors, satellite imagery, weather forecasts, market indicators, and regulatory signals.

D. State-Space Risk Evolution

Risk evolution may be modeled through hidden Markov or continuous state-space structures, allowing the system to estimate latent risk states from noisy observations over time.

State Transition: captures physical process dynamics and the effect of governance interventions.

Observation Layer: maps sensor, market, regulatory, and environmental signals to latent system states.

Filtering Methods: Kalman filtering or particle filtering may be used for real-time state estimation and prediction.

This methodology remains conceptual. No validated system identification or operational calibration has been completed.

E. Real Options Valuation — Activation Timing

The decision to activate TFP protocols may be modeled as an option-like decision under uncertainty. The underlying governance question is whether acting earlier preserves reversibility value compared to delayed intervention.

In this structure, activation timing is assessed against avoided loss value, activation cost, information arrival, irreversibility deadlines, and uncertainty regarding system trajectory.

Governance Logic: delayed action may preserve optionality, but excessive delay can destroy reversibility.

Reversibility Premium: the economic value associated with maintaining the capacity to intervene before systemic degradation becomes irreversible.

Activation Boundary: the point at which expected avoided loss exceeds activation cost, adjusted for uncertainty and irreversibility.

G. Living Contract Architecture

Living Contracts are contractual frameworks with embedded compliance, monitoring, and activation clauses that adapt to regulatory and operational changes. They combine elements of smart-contract execution with legally enforceable traditional contract structures.

Contract State Machine: draft → negotiated → executed → monitoring → activated → resolved or disputed.

Auto-Adaptation Logic: regulatory change detection, operational threshold breach response, market condition renegotiation.

Legal Enforceability: jurisdiction selection, digital notarization, tiered dispute resolution, and force majeure correlation.

H. Systemic Containment Mechanisms

Systemic Containment Mechanisms are governance protocols intended to isolate and contain systemic risk propagation across operational domains. They function as circuit-breaker protocols for socio-ecological systems, analogous to financial market circuit breakers but applied to environmental and operational risk cascades.

Nodes: mining, energy, agriculture, logistics, finance, regulation, community, ecosystem.

Edges: supply chain dependency, water dependency, regulatory cascade, financial contagion, and social amplification.

Containment Actions: isolate node, redirect flows, amplify monitoring, notify stakeholders, mobilize reserves.

K. Limitations and Assumptions

Mathematical Limitations: Bayesian priors may be unavailable for novel contexts; state-space assumptions may not hold for catastrophic tail events; real-options valuation approximates discrete institutional reality.

Data Limitations: sensor networks may fail during extreme events; satellite data has resolution constraints; regulatory data may lag; market data may not reflect emerging risks.

Operational Limitations: automated activation may face legal challenge; stakeholder coordination requires trust; false activation costs may exceed benefits in low-probability contexts.

Epistemic Limitations: unknown unknowns cannot be modeled; model drift requires continuous recalibration; stakeholder preferences may change faster than model updates.
