PREAMBLE

International Statute on Systemic Governance, Predictive Legal Coordination, and Biophysical Limits
The c-ECO Systemic Governance Statute establishes a transnational legal framework for predictive governance in contexts of systemic, cumulative, and potentially irreversible risk, recognizing that the validity, efficacy, and continuity of legal and economic relations depend upon the material conditions that sustain life, economic activity, and the functional integrity of the Earth System.
WHEREAS, contemporary legal systems have progressively incorporated anticipatory instruments — including precautionary principles, due diligence obligations, risk-based regulation, and macroprudential supervision — designed to address risk prior to the materialization of harm;
WHEREAS, notwithstanding these developments, such anticipatory regimes remain structurally insufficient under conditions of systemic complexity, as they are predominantly probabilistic, sectorally fragmented, and oriented toward the management of risk as potential harm rather than toward the preservation of systemic reversibility;
WHEREAS, systemic risks are characterized by cumulative effects, feedback dynamics, non-linear propagation, and temporal asymmetries in which the window for effective intervention may close prior to the manifestation of legally cognizable damage;
WHEREAS, under such conditions, formally valid and fully compliant acts, contracts, and operations may generate trajectories that progressively erode the biophysical and systemic conditions upon which their own viability depends;
RECOGNIZING the emergence of Trajectory Illegality, whereby formally lawful acts may become legally incompatible with systemic stability when their technically foreseeable continuation leads toward ecosystem degradation, critical infrastructure disruption, or compromise of the global commons;
AFFIRMING that the economic value, validity, and enforceability of legal bonds must remain materially connected to the preservation of the Safe Operating Space (SOS), the habitability of ecosystems, and the technical reversibility of risk trajectories;
ESTABLISHING the central relevance of certified technical data, continuous monitoring, and auditable systemic evidence as essential elements for anticipatory legal coordination, while preserving the role of legal institutions, due process, and structured technical review;
ESTABLISHING the Pre-Threshold Principle as a foundational juridical rule, whereby juridical relevance attaches not upon the materialization of harm nor exclusively upon the crossing of critical thresholds, but at the point at which the remaining capacity for reversibility begins to contract in a measurable and technically verifiable manner;
INSTITUTING a systemic juridical architecture through which the validity, enforceability, and continuity of legal and economic relations are conditioned upon their ongoing compatibility with biophysical stability and the preservation of reversibility, operationalized through, inter alia, Ex-Ante Unenforceability (IEX), systemic perclusion, the conditioned immediate object, functional conversion, Systemic Proof, and the Pre-Threshold Principle;
DECLARING that, within the scope of this Statute, biophysical reality and the temporal constraints of systemic processes shall guide legal interpretation wherever procedural delay, institutional inertia, or formally valid execution would contribute to irreversible harm or systemic destabilization;
THIS STATUTE SOLEMNLY AFFIRMS THAT:
I — No legal or economic arrangement may be treated as autonomous from the material conditions that render its performance possible;
II — No profit or economic advantage shall be protected where it depends upon systemic exhaustion, irreversible degradation, or the destruction of the life-support conditions on which legal and economic order itself depends;
III — The preservation of the functional integrity of the Earth System constitutes a legally relevant limit for the organization, interpretation, and execution of contracts, assets, operations, and infrastructures under this Statute.
IV — The compatibility of legal acts with systemic reversibility constitutes a condition for their continued validity and enforceability.
V — Juridical relevance shall attach at the pre-threshold stage, defined as the condition in which measurable contraction of reversibility indicates that continued inertial execution may compromise the possibility of stabilization or recovery.
By the authority of these principles, the c-ECO Systemic Governance Regime is hereby instituted as a Model Law and transnational governance framework, designed to guide contracts, assets, operations, and infrastructures whose execution interacts with critical systemic boundaries, with the objective of preventing irreversible destabilization, preserving reversibility, enabling regeneration, and aligning juridical execution with the material conditions that sustain it.

EXPLANATORY MEMORANDUM

On the Structural Limits of Contemporary Anticipatory Law and the Emergence of Trajectory-Based Legal Conditioning
Contemporary legal systems have undergone a significant transformation through the incorporation of anticipatory instruments designed to govern risk prior to the materialization of harm. Preventive regulation, precautionary principles, due diligence obligations, and systemic risk supervision have expanded the temporal reach of law, enabling intervention under uncertainty and before the occurrence of damage.
Existing ex-ante regimes are predominantly oriented toward the identification, assessment, and mitigation of risk conceived as the probability of adverse events. They operate within frameworks that remain event-centered, probabilistic, and sectorally bounded. While they enhance the capacity of legal systems to respond to uncertainty, they do not fully address the dynamics through which complex systems evolve toward states of reduced or exhausted reversibility.
In systemic contexts, the critical juridical problem is not merely the occurrence of harm, but the progressive loss of the capacity to prevent, interrupt, or reverse trajectories before harm becomes observable or legally actionable. The temporal window for effective intervention may close prior to the manifestation of damage, rendering traditional and even advanced anticipatory responses insufficient to preserve system integrity.
Within this context, this Statute recognizes the emergence of Trajectory Illegality.
Trajectory illegality designates the condition in which formally lawful acts, when executed in an inertial or unconditioned manner, generate technically foreseeable trajectories that lead toward systemic degradation, infrastructural destabilization, or the exhaustion of reversibility. The incompatibility does not arise from normative violation or realized harm, but from the projected evolution of the act within a system subject to material limits. This requires a reconfiguration of juridical categories.
The classical distinction between lawfulness and unlawfulness is supplemented by a third dimension: the systemic compatibility of the execution trajectory. Legal evaluation is no longer confined to the moment of the act or to the occurrence of its effects, but extends to the dynamic relation between legal operations and the limits of the systems within which they unfold.
The c-ECO Regime articulates this transformation through a shift from risk-based anticipation to trajectory-based legal conditioning.
Within this framework, the validity, enforceability, and continuity of legal bonds are conditioned upon their ongoing compatibility with the Safe Operating Space and the availability of reversibility, as determined through objective, verifiable, and continuously updated technical evidence.
To operationalize this transition, the Statute establishes a coordinated set of structural legal mechanisms.
Ex-Ante Unenforceability (IEX) functions as a constitutive filter through which claims that remain formally valid but have become incompatible with systemic stability are deprived of enforceability.
Systemic perclusion operates as an immediate juridical interruption of acts whose continuation would advance trajectories toward irreversible states, preventing inertial execution under conditions of systemic risk.
The conditioned immediate object links the existence and enforceability of legal obligations to the continued integrity of the systems that sustain their performance, enabling suspension, adaptation, or functional transformation in response to material instability.
Systemic Proof establishes an evidentiary regime grounded in certified sensory data, continuous monitoring, and auditable technical validation, enabling the production of immediate juridical effects aligned with the temporal dynamics of the system.
These instruments are preventive and conservatory in nature. They do not primarily operate through sanction, but through the conditioning of legal effects in order to preserve systemic integrity, maintain material public order, and ensure the continued viability of the Earth System under conditions of measurable and evolving risk.
