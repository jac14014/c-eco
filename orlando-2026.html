Seeing the Invisible: Systemic Risks, Expanded Due Diligence, and Pre-Threshold Contractual Governance

Structural Displacements
The Structural Problem of Law Facing Systemic Risk
The Temporal Asymmetry of Legal Response
Contemporary law already incorporates anticipatory instruments: precaution, due diligence, risk-based regulation, systemic supervision. However, a structural asymmetry persists:
  • The time of legal response is not the time of systemic deterioration;
  • Judicial response is episodic, while degradation is continuous;
  • Legal relevance arrives at damage or imminence, while reversibility may be lost before.
The c-ECO framework addresses this asymmetry by creating an architecture in which monitored trajectory and reversibility become capable of producing pre-structured legal effects before irreversible deterioration consolidates.
Purpose of This Article
This article establishes the theoretical foundations of the c-ECO framework—from the epistemology of collapse to pre-threshold governance, from expanded due diligence to algorithmic legitimacy. It aims to demonstrate that law already sees fragments, but not systems; already monitors, but does not transform measurement into a structuring criterion of exigibility.
1. The Epistemology of Collapse: Why Law Sees Fragments and Not Systems
1.1 The processual nature of systemic disasters
Proper understanding of the c-ECO proposal requires a break with an epistemologically limited view of the nature of systemic disasters. Contrary to the popular image of sudden and unpredictable collapses, the literature on complex systems demonstrates that the vast majority of disasters are preceded by gradual processes of deterioration, accumulation of vulnerabilities, and perfectly detectable technical stress signals. This finding shifts the focus from the question "why can't we predict?" to "why, having elements of predictability, can't we act preventively?"
Gradual Deterioration

Progressive deviations that accumulate below the perception threshold of traditional governance structures, configured to respond to discrete events rather than continuous degradation processes.

Accumulation of Vulnerabilities

Technically complex and coupled systems tend to normalize progressive deviations (Dekker's "drift into failure") until a trivial event triggers irreversible cascade.

Technical Stress Signals

Stress indicators that, if properly monitored and integrated into decision-making protocols, would have allowed interventions in windows of opportunity that closed until irreversibility.

Reversibility Window

The zone of ambiguity between "everything works adequately" and "irreversible collapse"—where recovery capacity is in measurable contraction, though not yet exhausted.

Paradigmatic Cases
From Bhopal (1984) to Fukushima (2011), from the 2008 financial crisis to contemporary environmental collapse: the history of systemic disasters is, to a large extent, the history of visible technical signals that remained legally unseen.
1.2 Collapse mechanisms: coupling, tolerance, tail, and normalization
The interdisciplinary literature identifies four fundamental mechanisms that operate synergistically in generating disasters: systemic coupling, low error tolerance, systematic underestimation of tail events, and progressive normalization of deviations. Each mechanism deserves analysis for its implications for the legal architecture proposed by c-ECO.
Systemic Coupling
Degree of interdependence between system components. Failure in one element propagates rapidly (Charles Perrow, Normal Accidents, 1984). Systems with high complexity and tight coupling are inherently prone to "normal accidents."
Low Error Tolerance
Safety margins progressively reduced in pursuit of efficiency. High Reliability Organizations (HROs) maintain "chronic unease" about failures—in opposition to the complacency of deviation normalization (Weick & Sutcliffe).
Tail Underestimation
Systematic tendency of risk models to underestimate rare high-magnitude events (Taleb). Structurally incentivized by regimes that reward short-term performance and penalize prudence.
Normalization of Deviations
Deviations gradually accepted as normal when they do not result in immediate consequences. Each deviation redefines the reference of "normality" (Diane Vaughan, The Challenger Launch Decision).
1.3 The illusion of opacity: the visible that remains legally unseen
Faced with these well-documented mechanisms, a paradox of first-order epistemological importance emerges: systemic disasters are, to a large extent, visible before they occur, but remain legally invisible. This invisibility is not an ontological property of risks, but a consequence of the categorical architecture of law—configured to operate based on discrete, qualifiable, and attributable events.
The Visible That Remains Unseen
The "illusion of opacity" consists in the belief that, because law cannot operate with certain phenomena, these phenomena are intrinsically opaque. This illusion exempts from responsibility actors who could have acted preventively and legitimizes legal regimes that respond only post factum. c-ECO proposes importing into contractual law the capacity to "see the invisible"—making legally operable information that already exists, but which traditional architecture cannot process.
2. Law Already Sees: Antecedents and Limits of the Current Structure
2.1 The relational revolution and its reaches
The c-ECO proposal does not emerge in a theoretical vacuum. It inserts itself into an intellectual trajectory dating back to the "relational revolution" in contract theory, associated primarily with Ian Macneil's work from the 1960s onward.
Discrete vs. Relational Contracts

Macneil (The New Social Contract, 1980): from discrete act to continuous social relation. Relational contracts involve exchange over time, incomplete specifications, interdependence, and context-dependence.

Good Faith and Adaptation

Good faith imposes duties of information, cooperation, and adaptation. In systems with revision for supervening excessive onerousness, it operates as a foundation for rebalancing. c-ECO anticipates this logic with continuous modulation.

Processual Key

First-order epistemological conquest: the contractual bond can be thought in processual key, not merely static.

Practical Stagnation

The processual key remained predominantly interpretative. No contractual infrastructure operates continuously based on processuality. c-ECO fills this gap.

2.2 Contractual governance based on monitoring: paradigmatic examples
Law has already developed continuous monitoring mechanisms in multiple domains. The question is not absence, but sectoral fragmentation and lack of integrated architecture.
Mechanism Function Limit
Financial Covenants (Smith & Warner, 1979) Continuous vigilance via observable accounting metrics; proxies for debtor behavior; anticipatory reaction before default Confined to corporate credit; standardized metrics not transferable to other systemic risks
UCC 2-609 — Adequate Assurance Operation before breach; prospective risk assessment; suspension of performance as protection "Reasonable insecurity" is subjective; binary response (guarantees or repudiation) without gradation; focus on other party, not on system
Precautionary Principle Normative foundation for action before certainty of damage Diffuse operationalization in contract law; remains as general guiding principle
Prudential Supervision Periodic reporting requirements, stress tests, graduated intervention Sectoral (financial); monitoring as management instrument, not as foundation of exigibility
Precise Diagnosis
The problem is not blindness of law to continuous risk, but structural myopia: capacity to focus details in specific domains, inability to integrate dispersed mechanisms into coherent architecture. c-ECO does not invent monitoring; it proposes its elevation to structuring criterion of exigibility.
3. Expanded Due Diligence: From Point Investigation to Governance Infrastructure
3.1 The traditional paradigm of due diligence
Traditional due diligence operates predominantly in informational and ex ante key. Its objective is to raise, before celebration, information about the counterparty's situation. Its paradigm is retrospective and static: it seeks to know what has already occurred and what exists at the moment.
Ex Ante Informational Key

Past facts, known risks, declared contingencies. Methods: document analysis, interviews, public records, financial statements.

Risk Allocation

Allows price adjustment, protection clauses (representations, warranties, indemnities), precedent conditions. Logic of ex ante optimization.

Investigative and Point Character

Occurs between intention to contract and celebration. Output: report of "what was found." New informational asymmetry emerges continuously during execution.

Epistemological Limits

Focus on verifiable past facts leaves out emerging dynamics. Does not internalize temporality of systemic risk. Risk is treated as property of state at given moment, not as process.

3.2 The displacement proposed by c-ECO
Expanded due diligence displaces the fundamental axis: from raising past facts to continuous monitoring of systemic conditions. This displacement is qualitative: it changes the type of knowledge from retrospective to prospective, from static to dynamic, from point to continuous.
1
Fact Raising

Point investigation of the past; photograph of a moving system

2
Continuous Monitoring

Accompaniment of systemic conditions; prior identification of relevant variables; tracking protocols

3
Performance Continuity

Object of active vigilance; captures second and third-order dependencies; example: systemic water stress in irrigation contract

4
Governance Infrastructure

Conversion into institutional structure; creation of clear responsibilities; ceases to be preparatory activity to become obligatory component

5
Metrics and Protocols

Previously integrated into the obligatory relation in three dimensions: contractual, technical, institutional

3.3 Theoretical foundations and overcoming limits
Incomplete contract theory (Hart, Grossman, Moore, 1980s) offers partial support. If contracts are necessarily incomplete, adaptation mechanisms are rational. But the proposed mechanisms are typically reactive: renegotiation when unforeseen contingency materializes.
Incomplete Contracts

Do not anticipate all contingencies due to transaction costs and cognitive limits. Adaptation mechanisms: renegotiation, residual control rights. "Partial" support because it treats incompleteness as problem, not opportunity.

Beyond Renegotiation

Opportunistic renegotiation and late intervention produce suboptimal results. More vulnerable party, already affected by deterioration, is compelled to accept unfavorable terms. Judicial intervention is invariably late.

Pre-Integrated Metrics

Metrics and protocols integrated into the obligatory relation in three dimensions: (i) contractual, (ii) technical, (iii) institutional. Distinguishes expanded due diligence from mere revision clause.

Institutionalized Anticipation

Not anticipation of specific contingencies (impossible), but of the need for adaptation as such, with protocols that structure it continuously.

4. Pre-Threshold Governance: Acting Within the Reversibility Window
4.1 The problem of legal temporality in responding to systemic risks
Traditional contract law is structurally configured to wait: for the breach, for supervening impossibility, for collapse. This waiting is constitutive of the logic of exigibility. The costs of this waiting are very high.
Waiting for Breach — Opportunity Costs and Irreversibility
  • Opportunity cost: effective low-cost interventions in the reversibility window become prohibitively expensive or impossible after consolidation of rupture;
  • Irreversibility cost: some systemic deteriorations are not recoverable, so that ex post response is necessarily incomplete;
  • Attribution cost: the later the response, the more difficult to attribute responsibilities, generating prolonged litigation and inefficiency.
4.2 The concept of reversibility window
The central concept is the "reversibility window": the zone in which the system can still be preserved, but its recovery capacity is already in measurable contraction. It is not yet impossibility (the system works), but it is no longer full viability (the system is deteriorating).
Preservation Zone

System can still be preserved, but recovery capacity in measurable contraction. Requires: indicator identification, baselines, contraction thresholds, response protocols.

States vs. Events

Fundamental difference: pre-threshold governance operates around systemic states (objective, measurable, continuous), not events or bilateral suspicions. Does not depend on subjective perception, but on previously agreed indicators.

Pre-Rupture Operation

Modulation of obligation intensity; activation of systemic guarantees; mitigation duties; temporary suspension of obligations that would aggravate deterioration; reactivation upon recovery.

Liquid Reversibility (Lr)

Measures whether immediately mobilizable resources exist to reverse or contain damaging trajectories in the relevant time window. Critical component of TFP.

4.3 Implications for contract law theory
Pre-threshold governance implies a displacement of focus from reparation to preservation as the primary function of contract law in contexts of systemic risk. The traditional paradigm seeks restitutio in integrum ex post; pre-threshold governance seeks maintenance of possibility conditions ex ante and continuously.
Teleological Displacement
It changes the purpose attributed to contract law: from instrument of risk allocation and damage reparation to instrument of governance of interdependent relations under conditions of systemic uncertainty. The "pre-threshold band" must be recognized as legitimate and necessary.
5. IEX — Ex Ante Inexigibility: Radicalization of a Latent Tendency
5.1 Dogmatic antecedents: the dispersed recognition of inexigibility by material states
IEX should not be presented as a category emerging from nowhere. Law already knows, in multiple traditions, hypotheses in which exigibility is affected by material states. The history is non-linear: periods of expansion and restriction, but general tendency of increasing recognition.
Tradition Legal Figure Characteristic
Common Law (USA) Impracticability (UCC 2-615); Frustration (Restatement 265) Performance unexecutable due to event whose non-occurrence was basic presupposition
French Law Théorie de l'imprévision Changes of circumstances affect exigibility; revision for supervening excessive onerousness
German Law Wegfall der Geschäftsgrundlage (313 BGB) Revision when fundamental presuppositions change; adaptation or extinction
Italian Law Eccessiva onerosità sopravvenuta (art. 1467 c.c.) Adaptation in cases of supervening excessive onerousness
Brazilian Law Art. 478, I, CC Revision for supervening excessive onerousness; resolution or modification
Transnational Law UNIDROIT (arts. 6.2.1-6.2.3); PECL (art. 6:111) Systematization at transnational level; hardship and obligatory renegotiation
5.2 The reorganization proposed by c-ECO
The point is not to deny antecedents, but to propose reorganization: exigibility ceases to depend only on qualifiable legal event and can be modulated by continuously monitored systemic state.
Qualifiable Legal Event
Supervening excessive onerousness, force majeure, unforeseeable change of circumstances. Episodic ex post response. Event that legally qualifies to trigger effects.
Monitored Systemic State
Continuous measurement of system. Graduated modulation of exigibility. Can be linear or non-linear, univariate or multivariate.
Non-Substitution
IEX radicalizes and systematizes already present tendency. Preserves function of traditional mechanisms for unforeseen situations or where continuous response proved insufficient.
Three Dimensions
Temporal (ex post -> continuous); Ontological (event -> state); Architectural (exception -> structuring rule). Maintenance of existing dogmatics is conceptually necessary.
5.3 The architectural innovation: from episodic response to preventive architecture
The innovation would be to transform dispersed recognition into preventive and continuous architecture. It is systematization, not invention. c-ECO identifies a principle that already operates latently and makes it explicit, operational, and structuring.
Systematization, Not Invention
Systematization implies: (i) conceptual unification around the notion of systemic state; (ii) technical operationalization through metrics and thresholds; (iii) contractual structuring as modulating layer of exigibility. The systemic state as operational legal variable.
6. Shared Systemic Duty: From Interpretative Cooperation to Operationalized Duty
6.1 Antecedents in contemporary law
The Shared Systemic Duty should also not be described as if there were no antecedents. There are relevant precedents in multiple legal categories. The common point: predominantly interpretative character.
Objective Good Faith

In robust dimension, imposes duties of loyalty, information, and cooperation that transcend the literal clauses. Judge can invoke it to impose duties case by case.

Collateral Duties (Nebenpflichten)

Conduct obligations that are not the main obligation, but protect interests of the other party. Emerge from the contractual bond.

Relational Theory

Grounds cooperative duties that emerge from the very nature of the contractual relation. In contexts of interdependence, acquire special relevance (Netzverträge, supply chains).

Interpretative Character

Operate as principles of interpretation and foundations of judicial decisions, but not as operationalizable obligations with determinable content beforehand. Parties do not know precisely what duties will be in future situations.

6.2 The analytical gain of c-ECO
The analytical gain is in converting cooperation from predominantly interpretative category to more operational category, based on protocols and metrics.
1
Interpretative Cooperation

Judge invokes good faith to impose duty of information or cooperation case by case. Indetermination of content. Dependence on judicial activation.

2
Operational Category

Conversion to operational category based on protocols and metrics. Reduction of indetermination zone.

3
Technical Foundation

Protocols: who monitors, with what frequency, with what methodology. Metrics: which states trigger the duty, with what intensity, for how long.

4
Systemic Activation

Duty emerges from objective metrics of system state. Transforms diffuse obligation of result into specific obligation of means.

6.3 The defensible formulation: not creation, but operationalization
The strongest formulation is not to say that c-ECO creates the duty of cooperation, but that it seeks to technicize it and activate it in response to identifiable systemic states.
Qualitative Differential: Systemic Activation
The duty of cooperation already exists in various forms in various legal systems. Its limits are well known: indetermination, judicial dependence, vulnerability to opportunism, verification costs. The differential of c-ECO is systemic activation: the duty emerges from objective metrics, not from generic assessment of "good faith." Transforms diffuse obligation of result into specific obligation of means (execute preservation protocols under defined conditions).
7. Conditioned Immediate Object: Exigibility as Function of System State
7.1 The conceptual precision of the proposal
The argument is not that the contract ceases to exist, nor that it automatically loses validity. The proposal is more refined: the obligation remains, but its exigibility becomes functionally conditioned to the preservation of material conditions that make performance possible.
Existence vs. Exigibility

The contract continues to produce effects: bond, expectations, trust. The intensity of exigibility—the legal pressure for performance—is modulated by system state.

Functional Conditionality

Not traditional suspensive/resolutive condition (future and uncertain event). It is continuous conditionality: independent variable (state) and dependent variable (exigibility) in previously defined functional relation.

Graduated Modulation

In deterioration states: extended deadlines, diminished volumes, flexibilized specifications. In recovery states: reestablished or increased (compensation).

Multivariate and Configurable

Can be linear or non-linear, univariate or multivariate. Definition is matter of autonomy of will, within limits of reasonableness and public order.

7.2 Dialogue with and overcoming traditional doctrines
This formulation dialogues with doctrines on impossibility, change of circumstances, and basis of transaction. c-ECO goes beyond by proposing continuous modulation, not merely ex post recognition.
Doctrine Operation Limit
Supervening impossibility (art. 478 CC) Extinguishes obligation when performance becomes objectively impossible Only operates when impossibility is already consolidated; late response
Theory of basis of transaction (Geschäftsgrundlage, 313 BGB) Allows revision when fundamental presuppositions change Ex post reaction to already materialized change
Rebus sic stantibus Adaptation for supervening excessive onerousness Requires qualifying event; episodic response; onerousness must be "excessive"
Hardship (UNIDROIT 6.2.1-6.2.3) Obligatory renegotiation in case of hardship Contingency materialization has already occurred; renegotiation under pressure
c-ECO / IEX Continuous modulation of exigibility by systemic state Requires pre-established contractual architecture; depends on measurability
7.3 The strong core of the thesis: material conditions of possibility
The preservation of material conditions of possibility of performance is prerequisite of its own exigibility. One cannot demand performance in a way that aggravates the deterioration of conditions that make it possible. Full exigibility presupposes sustainability of the system.
Exigibility = f(Systemic State, Metrics, Thresholds, Protocols)
Legal Functionalization
Measurement ceases to be merely technical or scientific activity and becomes component of the very legal structure of obligation. System state indicators are variables that enter into the definition of obligation content at each moment.
8. Systemic Guarantees: Functional Reordering of Existing Instruments
8.1 The prior legal inventory
It is important to avoid overclaim. Law already knows instruments that fulfill analogously oriented functions toward preservation of conditions—though in the current paradigm their function is predominantly reactive.
Instrument Current Function Activation Trigger
Performance Bonds Guarantee execution of work or service Verifiable breach
Environmental Bonds (Reclamation Bonds) Guarantee environmental recovery Configured environmental damage or due recovery obligation
Decommissioning Funds Finance facility decommissioning Due decommissioning obligation
Escrow Accounts Custody of resources for conditional release Pre-defined contractual event
8.2 The reordering proposed by c-ECO
The defensible point is to reorder the function of these instruments, displacing the activation trigger from configured breach/damage to measured deterioration of systemic conditions.
Displaced Trigger

From configured breach/damage to measured deterioration of systemic conditions. Not mere nomenclature change, but qualitative transformation.

Transformed Function

From insurance against breach risk (protects creditor) to investment in preservation of relation possibility conditions (protects both parties and the system).

Non-Invention

Functional redirection of already existing instruments, with reconfiguration of triggers, beneficiaries, and purpose.

Preservation of Relation

Maintenance of conditions that allow sustainable evolution of relation, including its transformation or orderly termination when conditions so require.

8.3 The qualitative transformation: from bilateral reaction to preservation of conditions
This reordering is not trivial. It transforms the guarantee from bilateral reaction mechanism to preservation mechanism of possibility conditions of the relation itself.
Implications for Obligation Theory
  • Focus displacement: from forced execution (how to make the debtor perform) to governance of conditions (how to preserve the system in which performance is possible);
  • Redistribution of responsibilities: not only the debtor is responsible for performance, but both parties (and potentially third parties) have systemic preservation responsibilities;
  • Different temporality: the guarantee is not mobilized only at the moment of failure, but continuously, as part of a governance regime.
9. Algorithmic Legitimacy: Seeing Without Arbitrariness
9.1 The dilemma of data-based governance
The closer legal governance approaches data, metrics, and automation, the stronger the requirement of institutional legitimacy becomes. c-ECO recognizes the risks and seeks to confront them in a structured manner.
Legal-Metric Approximation

Growing tendency of linkage between legal governance, technical metrics, and automation. Proportional requirement of legitimacy.

Contemporary Critique

Pasquale (The Black Box Society, 2015): scoring systems operate with inaccessible algorithms. O'Neil (Weapons of Math Destruction, 2016): neutral models reproduce inequalities.

Hidden Arbitrariness

Metrics and algorithms, by apparent objectivity, escape critical scrutiny. Objectivity is illusory: depends on design choices invisibilized by complexity.

c-ECO Risk

Cannot be defended as simple automation. Defensibility depends on four structural conditions that incorporate accountability into its own architecture.

9.2 The four conditions of c-ECO defensibility
Defensibility depends on four conditions that ensure that metrics-based governance does not become tyranny of numbers.
Auditability
Measurement systems susceptible to independent review. How metrics were chosen, calculated, verified, how they evolve over time. Prevents "objectivity" from being shield against questioning.
Governance
Supervision instance with capacity to interpret metrics, resolve conflicts, decide on unforeseen cases. Essential component of legitimacy.
Reversibility
Decisions based on metrics must be susceptible to review and reversal. Insurance against inevitable errors in any measurement system.
Transparency
Parties and affected third parties must have access to information about system operation. Sufficient for understanding and contestation. Does not imply total publicity (commercially sensitive information).
9.3 The redistribution of institutional mediation
Institutional mediation does not disappear in c-ECO; it is redistributed. Instead of concentration in judge or arbitrator ex post, there is dispersion in multiple instances: measurement systems, technical verification bodies, contractual governance instances.
Immanent Accountability
Accountability is not external addition to c-ECO, but immanent to its operationalization. The proposal is only defensible if it incorporates, in its own architecture, the mechanisms that guarantee its legitimacy. It is not a choice between efficiency and legitimacy, but recognition that efficiency depends on legitimacy.
10. Conclusion: From Fragmentation to Systematization — The Nature of c-ECO's Contribution
10.1 What c-ECO is not: Scope delimitation
The contribution of c-ECO does not lie in claiming that contemporary law ignores risk, monitoring, or continuous dynamics. On the contrary, it begins from the recognition that such instruments already operate with a high degree of sophistication across multiple legal domains. Denying this foundation would not only be inaccurate — it would undermine the internal coherence of the proposal.
Not Normative Blindness

Contemporary law already incorporates advanced monitoring mechanisms, particularly in financial, regulatory, and infrastructural sectors. c-ECO does not emerge to “correct an absence,” but to reorganize an existing capacity under a new juridical criterion: systemic trajectory.

Not an Invention ex nihilo

Its structural elements correspond to well-established legal constructs — conditional clauses, covenants, duties of diligence, review mechanisms, and compliance structures. The innovation lies not in the individual components, but in their integrated articulation into a coherent regime oriented by systemic limits.

Not a substitution of existing doctrine

c-ECO neither displaces nor invalidates traditional contractual doctrine. It operates as an additional layer that reconfigures the function of existing legal mechanisms, without eliminating their applicability. The logic is not one of rupture, but of functional recalibration under conditions of systemic risk.

Not an operational utopia

Continuous monitoring is already a consolidated economic reality — particularly through financial covenants, performance metrics, and contractual triggers. What c-ECO proposes is the extension of this rationality beyond the financial domain, into areas where the erosion of reversibility remains legally unaddressed.

10.2 What c-ECO is: the potential contribution
The potential contribution lies in reorganizing existing elements as structural criterion of contractual exigibility. It is integration of dispersed mechanisms into new architecture.
Synthesis, Not Invention
c-ECO is synthesis: it synthesizes learnings from multiple domains (financial law, environmental law, regulatory law, contract theory, systems science) into a coherent and transversal architecture. It does not add new blocks, but reconfigures the structure that sustains them.
10.3 The fundamental displacement
The intended displacement can be synthesized in three convergent movements.
1
From Fragmentation to Systematization

Sectoral and non-communicating mechanisms (each domain partially reinvents solutions) -> Integrated architecture with cross-learning, metrics standardization, and protocol scalability

2
From Event to State

Application triggered by qualifiable legal events (breach, impossibility, excessive onerousness) -> Applicability conditioned by continuously measured systemic state

3
Continuity as Foundation

Continuity of systemic measurement becomes operational foundation of exigibility modulation, with protocols, metrics, thresholds, and governance instances that make it legally effective
