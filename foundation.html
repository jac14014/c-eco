About Origins Support

About

The Foundation supports approaches that integrate scientific knowledge, institutional design, and legal architecture before irreversible loss occurs. It is not an advocacy organization. Its role is to safeguard intellectual independence, methodological rigor, and long-term continuity across interdisciplinary work bridging law, science, and governance.

Intellectual Orientation

The Foundation draws inspiration from continental European traditions in which law, science, and public institutions were conceived as interdependent systems operating across extended temporal horizons—where responsibility is distributed across time and institutional continuity matters.

Relationship to the c-ECO Doctrine

The Foundation provides institutional support for research and initiatives aligned with the c-ECO Doctrine (Contractual Equity & Ecological Co-Responsibility), which translates Earth-system constraints and systemic risk into ex ante legal and contractual decision frameworks.

Origins of the c-ECO Doctrine

The work of Jacqueline A. Ennis, author of the c-ECO Doctrine, is rooted in a lineage in which law, science, territory, and moral responsibility were historically treated as interdependent structures. This is not a genealogy of prestige, but a continuity of method: systems understood as living, temporal, and constrained by limits that precede individual will.

The “memory of life” is a unifying thread in this lineage: systems do not restart at each decision point. They carry accumulated histories—biological, ecological, and institutional—that delimit what remains possible.

The Weber Foundation: Cosmology and Systemic Reason

With Andreas Weber (1718–1781), a rationalist philosopher and Lutheran theologian, the lineage adopts a view in which nature, morality, law, and theology form a single intelligible order. In c-ECO terms, this anticipates systemic memory: the idea that systems have internal constraints shaped by accumulated structure over time.

From Philosophy to Science: Georg Heinrich Weber and Biological Memory

With Georg Heinrich Weber (1752–1828), medicine and botany translate systemic thinking into empirical practice. Living systems retain traces of past conditions and stresses—what c-ECO later frames as forms of biological memory shaping resilience and thresholds.

Law as Institutional Architecture: Johann Christian Hasse

With Johann Christian Hasse (1779–1830), law appears as an ex ante institutional architecture. Institutions “remember”: doctrine, precedent, and legal form carry the past forward and constrain future options. c-ECO extends this logic by embedding ecological thresholds and irreversibility into legal and contractual frameworks.

Crossing the Atlantic: Eduard Hass and Ecological Memory in the Amazon

With Eduard Johannes Heinrich Hass (1820–1880), systemic thinking meets territory. In nineteenth-century Belém (Amazon region), urban order, public health, drainage, and arborization become inseparable from climate and ecology. In c-ECO terms, this is ecological memory: interventions leave durable traces in landscapes and systems, shaping future constraints.

Conceptual Parallels to c-ECO

The Foundation’s historical references are invoked not as claims of direct doctrinal lineage, but as a disciplined acknowledgement of methodological affinities—how law and science can operate across time, structure responsibility, and respect systemic limits.

Johann Christian Hasse ↔ c-ECO

  • Ex ante structure: law designed as architecture, not reaction.
  • Institutional temporality: responsibility distributed across time and doctrine.
  • Continuity: durable legal forms that constrain future options.

Georg Heinrich Weber ↔ c-ECO

  • Systems as living networks: interdependence and fragile equilibria.
  • Biological memory: past pressures shape present resilience and thresholds.
  • Empirical discipline: observation informing governance design.

Eduard Hass ↔ c-ECO

  • Territory as system: city, climate, water cycles, vegetation, and health as one.
  • Ecological memory: interventions leave long-term traces and constraints.
  • Infrastructure as governance: arborization, drainage, and green systems as public order.

Memory of Life (c-ECO)

  • Ecological memory: ecosystems retain the imprint of past stress and change.
  • Institutional memory: law stores commitments, precedents, and path dependencies.
  • Operational translation: thresholds, indicators, and ex ante decision constraints.
