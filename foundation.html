Institutions Are Living Structures

Johann Christian Hasse understood law not as a reactive apparatus triggered only after harm, but as a temporal and institutional architecture. Courts, doctrines, and universities carry memory, constrain futures, and shape conduct long before damage is visible.

The Foundation is named after Hasse not to build a memorial, but to activate a method: law as structure, responsibility as continuity, and governance as the careful handling of thresholds.

This editorial series explores a central idea: under conditions of systemic risk, institutional “neutrality” is itself an intervention. When the window for meaningful intervention is temporal and finite, law must become ex ante—capable of organizing responsibility before irreversibility.

“Time is memory. Systems carry history. And law must govern not only actions, but their irreversible consequences.”

Dossier

A Family of Thought

A lineage of method (not prestige): systems thinking, institutional leadership, and the translation of living constraints into governance.

Andreas Weber (1718–1781)

Philosopher and theologian; university governance including Vice-Rector roles. A rational cosmology where coherence and limits precede choice.

Georg Heinrich Weber (1752–1828)

Physician and botanist; professor and Rector at the University of Kiel; founder of hospital infrastructure and the Botanical Garden. Living systems as networks.

Johann Christian Hasse (1779–1830)

Jurist; professor and Rector at the University of Bonn; law as institutional architecture operating through time—ex ante responsibility.

Peter Gottlieb Eduard Puggé (1802–1836)

Historical School of Law; professor of Karl Marx; tragedy and the limits of reason: systems exert pressure and can overwhelm.

Georg Weber (1818–1891)

Physician; political engagement and correspondence with Marx; transatlantic period in North America; structures can oppress, fracture, or collapse.

Eduard Hass (moved to Brazil at 26; died at 36)

Technical practitioner rather than professor: carried systemic intelligence into Amazonian territory and died young from tuberculosis contracted in the course of work.

Field Notes

Belém as a Living City

Hass’s work demonstrates urban ecology as governance: trees, water, and shade as living infrastructure.

Praça Batista Campos — the Garden City in practice

Hass turned the former “Praça Sergipe” into the celebrated Praça Batista Campos by applying an English Garden style: organic paths, water circulation, canopy-driven microclimates, and cast-iron civic elements integrated into a living system.

  • • Organic landscapes: winding paths, artificial lakes, rustic bridges (rocaille technique)
  • • Microclimate: dense canopies to lower local temperatures
  • • Hydraulic design: connected streams and fountains shaping sound and humidity
  • • Industrial art: bandstands (coretos), “Chafariz das Sereias,” and the “Castelinho”

Other Hass landmarks

Praça da República
Civic landscape around Theatro da Paz: spatial order + shade.
Horto Municipal
Botanical laboratory + nursery for urban arborization.
“City of Mango Trees”
Green corridors as thermal regulation infrastructure.

Rupture and the Limits of Reason

Puggé’s life and death remind us: systems organize life, but they also exert pressure. Memory includes rupture.

Puggé (1802–1836): historical law, justice, tragedy

A representative of the Historical School of Law and professor of Karl Marx, Puggé taught constitutional law, international law, legal philosophy, methodology, and natural law. His sudden death—reported widely—entered the lineage as a signal of irreversibility: not every system can absorb ethical pressure without fracture.
